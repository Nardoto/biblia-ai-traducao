# Luke 20

**1** One day, while Jesus was teaching the people in the temple courts and proclaiming the good news, the chief priests and the scribes, along with the elders, confronted him. **2** "Tell us," they said, "by what authority are you doing these things? Who gave you this authority?"

**3** He answered them, "I'll ask you a question too. Tell me: **4** John's baptism — was it from heaven, or from human origin?"

**5** They discussed it among themselves: "If we say, 'From heaven,' he'll say, 'Then why didn't you believe him?' **6** But if we say, 'From human origin,' all the people will stone us, because they're convinced John was a prophet." **7** So they answered that they didn't know where it was from.

**8** Jesus said, "Then I'm not telling you by what authority I do these things either."

**9** He went on to tell the people this parable: "A man planted a vineyard, leased it out to tenant farmers, and went away for a long time.ᵃ **10** At harvest time, he sent a servant to the tenants so they'd give him his share of the vineyard's produce. But the tenants beat him and sent him away empty-handed. **11** He sent another servant, and they beat and humiliated that one too, sending him away empty-handed. **12** He sent still a third, and they wounded him and threw him out.

**13** "Then the owner of the vineyard said, 'What should I do? I'll send my son, the one I love.ᵇ Perhaps they'll respect him.'

**14** "But when the tenants saw him, they said to each other, 'This is the heir. Let's kill him, and the inheritance will be ours.' **15** So they threw him out of the vineyard and killed him.

"What then will the owner of the vineyard do to them? **16** He'll come and destroy those tenants and give the vineyard to others."

When the people heard this, they said, "May that never happen!"ᶜ

**17** Jesus looked straight at them and said, "Then what does this mean — what's written in Scripture:

'The stone the builders rejected
has become the cornerstone'?ᵈ

**18** "Everyone who falls on that stone will be broken to pieces, and anyone it falls on will be crushed."

**19** The scribes and chief priests wanted to seize him right then and there — they knew he'd told this parable against them. But they were afraid of the people.

**20** So they watched for their chance and sent spies who pretended to be sincere, hoping to catch him in something he said — so they could hand him over to the governor's authority and jurisdiction. **21** They asked him, "Teacher, we know that you speak and teach correctly, and you don't show favoritism but teach the way of God truthfully. **22** Is it lawful for us to pay taxes to Caesar, or not?"ᵉ

**23** He saw through their craftiness and said to them, **24** "Show me a denarius. Whose image and inscription are on it?"

"Caesar's," they said.

**25** He said, "Then give to Caesar what belongs to Caesar, and to God what belongs to God."ᶠ

**26** They couldn't trap him in anything he said in front of the people. Amazed by his answer, they fell silent.

**27** Then some Sadducees — who say there's no resurrection — came to him with a question.ᵍ **28** "Teacher," they said, "Moses wrote for us that if a man's brother dies, leaving a wife but no children, the man should marry the widow and produce offspring for his brother. **29** Now there were seven brothers. The first one married a woman and died childless. **30** The second **31** and then the third married her — and in the same way, all seven died without leaving children. **32** Finally, the woman died too. **33** So in the resurrection, whose wife will she be? All seven were married to her."

**34** Jesus answered, "The people of this age marry and are given in marriage. **35** But those who are considered worthy of taking part in that age and in the resurrection from the dead neither marry nor are given in marriage.ʰ **36** They can't die anymore, because they are like the angels. They are children of God, being children of the resurrection.

**37** "But that the dead are raised — even Moses showed this at the bush,ⁱ where he calls the Lord 'the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob.' **38** He is not the God of the dead but of the living, for to him all are alive."

**39** Some of the scribes responded, "Teacher, you've spoken well." **40** And they no longer dared to ask him anything.

**41** Then Jesus said to them, "How can they say that the Messiah is David's son? **42** David himself says in the book of Psalms:

'The Lord said to my Lord:
"Sit at my right hand
**43** until I make your enemies
a footstool for your feet."'ʲ

**44** "David calls him 'Lord' — so how can he be his son?"

**45** While all the people were listening, he said to his disciples, **46** "Watch out for the scribes. They love walking around in long robes and being greeted with respect in the marketplaces. They love the best seats in the synagogues and the places of honor at banquets. **47** They devour widows' houses and make a show of saying long prayers. These men will receive the severest punishment."

## Notes

- v.9a: The parable of the wicked tenants draws on Isaiah 5:1-7, the Song of the Vineyard. The vineyard is Israel; the tenants are its leaders; the servants are the prophets; the beloved son is Jesus. The allegory would have been transparent.
- v.13b: "τὸν υἱόν μου τὸν ἀγαπητόν" (ton huion mou ton agapēton) — "my son, the beloved one." This echoes the voice at Jesus's baptism (3:22) and transfiguration (9:35), making the identification of the son with Jesus unmistakable.
- v.16c: "μὴ γένοιτο" (mē genoito) — "May it never be!" This horrified response from the crowd is unique to Luke and shows they understood the parable's implications.
- v.17d: Psalm 118:22. "κεφαλὴν γωνίας" (kephalēn gōnias) — the "head of the corner," either the capstone (crowning stone) or the cornerstone (foundation stone). Either way, the rejected stone becomes the most important one.
- v.22e: The question is a political trap. Saying "yes" to paying taxes would alienate the people who resented Roman taxation. Saying "no" would constitute sedition against Rome.
- v.25f: "τὰ Καίσαρος... τὰ τοῦ θεοῦ" — Jesus doesn't merely dodge the trap. He establishes a principle: civil and divine obligations both exist, but they operate in different domains. The deeper question — what belongs to God? — is left for the hearer to answer (everything, since humans bear God's image as the coin bears Caesar's).
- v.27g: The Sadducees accepted only the Pentateuch (five books of Moses) as authoritative and denied resurrection, angels, and spirits (Acts 23:8). Their question is designed to make resurrection look absurd.
- v.35h: Jesus reframes the entire discussion. Resurrection life is not an extension of present life with its social structures; it is a qualitatively different existence.
- v.37i: "ἐπὶ τῆς βάτου" (epi tēs batou) — "at the bush," i.e., in the passage about the burning bush (Exodus 3:6). This citation method refers to Scripture sections by a notable event, as chapter and verse divisions didn't yet exist.
- v.42-43j: Psalm 110:1. If the Messiah were merely David's descendant, David wouldn't call him "Lord." The implication: the Messiah transcends Davidic lineage — he is both David's son and David's Lord.
