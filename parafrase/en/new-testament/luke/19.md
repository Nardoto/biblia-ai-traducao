# Luke 19

**1** Jesus entered Jericho and was passing through. **2** There was a man named Zacchaeus — a chief tax collector and very wealthy. **3** He was trying to see who Jesus was, but he couldn't because of the crowd, since he was short. **4** So he ran ahead and climbed a sycamore-fig treeᵃ to see him, because Jesus was going to pass that way.

**5** When Jesus reached the spot, he looked up and said, "Zacchaeus, come down immediately. I need to stay at your house today." **6** He came down at once and welcomed Jesus gladly.

**7** When the crowd saw this, they all grumbled, "He's gone to be the guest of a sinner."

**8** But Zacchaeus stood up and said to the Lord, "Look, Lord — I'm giving half of my possessions to the poor. And if I've cheated anyone out of anything, I'm paying back four times as much."ᵇ

**9** Jesus said to him, "Today, salvation has come to this house, because this man too is a son of Abraham.ᶜ **10** For the Son of Man came to seek and save the lost."

**11** While they were listening to this, he went on to tell a parable, because he was near Jerusalem and they supposed God's kingdom was going to appear immediately. **12** He said, "A man of noble birth went to a distant country to receive kingly authority and then return.ᵈ **13** He called ten of his servants and gave them ten minas.ᵉ 'Put this money to work,' he said, 'until I come back.'

**14** "But his citizens hated him and sent a delegation after him, saying, 'We don't want this man to reign over us.'

**15** "When he returned, having received the kingship, he summoned the servants he'd given the money to, so he could find out what they'd gained. **16** The first came and said, 'Sir, your mina has earned ten more minas.' **17** 'Well done, good servant!' he said. 'Because you've been faithful in a very small matter, you'll have authority over ten cities.'

**18** "The second came and said, 'Sir, your mina has produced five more minas.' **19** He said to this one too, 'You'll be over five cities.'

**20** "Then another came and said, 'Sir, here is your mina. I kept it stored away in a cloth, **21** because I was afraid of you — you're a hard man. You take out what you didn't put in, and reap what you didn't sow.'

**22** "He said to him, 'I'll judge you by your own words, you wicked servant! You knew I was a hard man, taking out what I didn't put in and reaping what I didn't sow? **23** Then why didn't you at least put my money in the bank, so when I came back I could have collected it with interest?'

**24** "Then he said to those standing by, 'Take his mina away from him and give it to the one who has ten.' **25** They said to him, 'Sir, he already has ten minas!'

**26** "'I'm telling you, everyone who has will be given more. But from the one who has nothing, even what they have will be taken away. **27** Now — as for those enemies of mine who didn't want me to be their king — bring them here and execute them in front of me.'"ᶠ

**28** After telling this, Jesus went on ahead, going up to Jerusalem.

**29** As he approached Bethphage and Bethany, at the place called the Mount of Olives, he sent two of his disciples ahead. **30** "Go to the village ahead of you," he said. "As you enter it, you'll find a colt tied there that no one has ever ridden. Untie it and bring it here. **31** If anyone asks you, 'Why are you untying it?' say, 'The Lord needs it.'"

**32** The two who were sent went and found it just as he had told them. **33** While they were untying the colt, its owners asked them, "Why are you untying the colt?"

**34** They said, "The Lord needs it."

**35** They brought it to Jesus, threw their cloaks on the colt, and helped Jesus onto it. **36** As he went along, people were spreading their cloaks on the road.ᵍ

**37** As he approached the road going down from the Mount of Olives, the whole crowd of disciples began to joyfully praise God in loud voices for all the mighty works they had seen:

**38** "Blessed is the king who comes in the name of the Lord!ʰ
Peace in heaven and glory in the highest!"

**39** Some of the Pharisees in the crowd said to him, "Teacher, rebuke your disciples!"

**40** He answered, "I'm telling you, if they keep quiet, the stones themselves will cry out."ⁱ

**41** As he drew near and saw the city, he wept over it,ʲ **42** saying, "If only you had recognized on this day what would bring you peace! But now it's hidden from your eyes. **43** The days will come when your enemies will build an embankment against you, surround you, and close in on you from every side. **44** They'll level you to the ground — you and your children within your walls. They won't leave one stone on top of another, because you didn't recognize the time of God's coming to you."ᵏ

**45** Then he entered the temple courts and began driving out those who were selling. **46** He said to them, "It is written: 'My house will be a house of prayer.' But you've turned it into a den of robbers."ˡ

**47** Every day he was teaching in the temple courts. The chief priests, the scribes, and the leaders of the people were trying to kill him, **48** but they couldn't find a way to do it, because all the people hung on every word he said.ᵐ

## Notes

- v.4a: "συκομορέαν" (sykomorean) — the sycamore-fig tree, common in Jericho. It had low branches, making it easy to climb — a practical detail that adds realism to the story. This entire episode is unique to Luke.
- v.8b: Zacchaeus's offer exceeds the law's requirements. Fourfold restitution was the penalty for stealing a sheep (Exodus 22:1). His generosity suggests genuine transformation, not mere guilt.
- v.9c: "υἱὸς Ἀβραάμ" (huios Abraam) — "a son of Abraham." Jesus affirms that Zacchaeus, despite being a chief tax collector (despised as a traitor and sinner), is still a legitimate member of God's people. Salvation restores his identity.
- v.12d: The historical backdrop is likely Archelaus, son of Herod the Great, who traveled to Rome in 4 BC to receive his kingdom, while a Jewish delegation followed to oppose him (Josephus, Antiquities 17.11.1). Jesus's audience in Jericho would have known this story well.
- v.13e: "μνᾶ" (mna) — a mina, worth about 100 denarii (roughly 100 days' wages). Far less than the talents in Matthew 25, making the parable's point about faithfulness in small things more pointed.
- v.27f: The harsh ending reflects the reality of ancient kingship, but in Jesus's parable it represents the final consequences of rejecting God's reign. Luke's version is notably more severe than Matthew's parable of the talents.
- v.36g: Luke omits the "palm branches" found in John 12:13. The spreading of cloaks echoes the anointing of Jehu as king (2 Kings 9:13).
- v.38h: Luke uniquely adds "the king" to the Psalm 118:26 quotation, making the messianic-royal claim explicit. The angels' song at Jesus's birth praised "peace on earth" (2:14); now the disciples cry "peace in heaven" — the circle of peace extends from heaven to earth.
- v.40i: A dramatic statement unique to Luke. The stones of Jerusalem — soon to be thrown down (v.44) — would testify if the people refused.
- v.41j: "ἔκλαυσεν" (eklausen) — Jesus wept. A different word from the weeping at Lazarus's tomb (John 11:35, edakrysen). This is audible sobbing. Only Luke records this scene of Jesus weeping over Jerusalem.
- v.44k: Jesus's prophecy of Jerusalem's destruction was fulfilled in devastating detail when Rome besieged and destroyed the city in AD 70. "τὸν καιρὸν τῆς ἐπισκοπῆς σου" (ton kairon tēs episkopēs sou) — "the time of your visitation." Episkopē means a divine visit for blessing — now tragically missed.
- v.46l: Combining Isaiah 56:7 and Jeremiah 7:11. The temple was meant for all nations to pray; instead it had become a marketplace serving the elite.
- v.48m: "ἐξεκρέματο αὐτοῦ ἀκούων" (exekremato autou akouōn) — the people "hung upon" him listening. A vivid verb (used only here in the NT) meaning to hang suspended, captivated. The crowds' devotion temporarily protects Jesus from the authorities.
