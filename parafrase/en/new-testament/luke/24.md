# Luke 24

**1** On the first day of the week, very early in the morning, the women went to the tomb, carrying the spices they had prepared. **2** They found the stone rolled away from the tomb, **3** but when they went inside, they didn't find the body of the Lord Jesus.ᵃ **4** While they were standing there, puzzled about this, suddenly two men in dazzling clothes stood beside them. **5** The women were terrified and bowed their faces to the ground. The men said to them, "Why are you looking for the living among the dead? **6** He isn't here — he has risen! Remember what he told you while he was still in Galilee: **7** 'The Son of Man must be handed over to sinful people, be crucified, and on the third day rise again.'"

**8** Then they remembered his words. **9** When they came back from the tomb, they reported all these things to the Eleven and to everyone else. **10** It was Mary Magdalene, Joanna, Mary the mother of James, and the others with them who told the apostles. **11** But their words seemed to the apostles like nonsense, and they didn't believe them.ᵇ **12** Peter, however, got up and ran to the tomb. He bent down, looked in, and saw nothing but the linen cloths. He went home, amazed at what had happened.ᶜ

**13** That same day, two of them were walking to a village called Emmaus, about seven miles from Jerusalem.ᵈ **14** They were talking with each other about everything that had happened. **15** While they were deep in conversation and discussion, Jesus himself came up and walked along with them — **16** but their eyes were kept from recognizing him.ᵉ

**17** He asked them, "What are you discussing as you walk along?"

They stopped, their faces downcast. **18** One of them, named Cleopas,ᶠ asked him, "Are you the only visitor to Jerusalem who doesn't know what's been happening there these past few days?"

**19** "What things?" he asked.

They said, "About Jesus of Nazareth. He was a prophet, powerful in word and deed before God and all the people. **20** The chief priests and our rulers handed him over to be sentenced to death, and they crucified him. **21** But we had hoped he was the one who was going to redeem Israel.ᵍ And on top of all this, it's now the third day since these things happened. **22** Then some women from our group astonished us. They went to the tomb early this morning **23** but didn't find his body. They came back and told us they'd seen a vision of angels who said he was alive. **24** Some of our companions went to the tomb and found it just as the women had described — but they didn't see him."

**25** He said to them, "How slow you are to understand — and how reluctant your hearts are to believe everything the prophets said! **26** Wasn't it necessary for the Messiahʰ to suffer these things and then enter his glory?" **27** And beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, he explained to them what was said about himself throughout all the Scriptures.ⁱ

**28** As they approached the village they were heading to, he acted as though he was going farther. **29** But they urged him strongly, "Stay with us — it's nearly evening, and the day is almost over." So he went in to stay with them.

**30** When he was at the table with them, he took the bread, blessed it, broke it, and gave it to them. **31** Then their eyes were opened, and they recognized him — and he vanished from their sight.ʲ

**32** They said to each other, "Weren't our hearts burning within us while he talked with us on the road and opened the Scriptures to us?"

**33** They got up that very hour and went back to Jerusalem. There they found the Eleven and their companions gathered together, **34** saying, "It's true! The Lord has risen! He has appeared to Simon!"ᵏ **35** Then the two told what had happened on the road and how they recognized him when he broke the bread.

**36** While they were still talking about this, Jesus himself stood among them and said, "Peace to you."ˡ

**37** They were startled and terrified, thinking they were seeing a ghost.ᵐ **38** He said to them, "Why are you troubled? Why do doubts arise in your hearts? **39** Look at my hands and my feet — it's really me! Touch me and see. A ghost doesn't have flesh and bones, as you can see I have." **40** After saying this, he showed them his hands and feet.

**41** While they still couldn't believe it — out of sheer joy and amazement — he asked them, "Do you have anything here to eat?" **42** They gave him a piece of broiled fish, **43** and he took it and ate it in front of them.ⁿ

**44** Then he said to them, "This is what I told you while I was still with you: everything written about me in the Law of Moses, the Prophets, and the Psalmsᵒ must be fulfilled."

**45** Then he opened their minds so they could understand the Scriptures. **46** He said to them, "This is what is written: the Messiah will suffer and rise from the dead on the third day, **47** and repentance for the forgiveness of sins will be proclaimed in his name to all nations, beginning from Jerusalem.ᵖ **48** You are witnesses of these things. **49** And I'm sending you what my Father promised. But stay in the city until you've been clothed with power from on high."ᑫ

**50** Then he led them out as far as Bethany. He lifted his hands and blessed them. **51** While he was blessing them, he parted from them and was carried up into heaven.ʳ **52** They worshiped him and returned to Jerusalem with overwhelming joy. **53** And they were continually in the temple, praising God.ˢ

## Notes

- v.3a: "τοῦ κυρίου Ἰησοῦ" (tou kyriou Iēsou) — "the Lord Jesus." This is the post-resurrection title, used by the narrator from the perspective of the early church's confession.
- v.11b: "ἐφάνησαν... ὡσεὶ λῆρος" (ephanēsan... hōsei lēros) — their words seemed like "lēros," a medical term meaning delirium or nonsense. The apostles' initial disbelief is candidly preserved — they did not come to faith easily.
- v.12c: This verse is absent from some manuscripts and may be a later harmonization with John 20:3-10. However, Peter's visit to the tomb is confirmed by the reference in v.24.
- v.13d: "σταδίους ἑξήκοντα" (stadious hexēkonta) — sixty stadia, approximately seven miles (11 km). The Emmaus road narrative (vv.13-35) is one of Luke's most beautifully crafted literary compositions, unique to his Gospel.
- v.16e: "οἱ δὲ ὀφθαλμοὶ αὐτῶν ἐκρατοῦντο" (hoi de ophthalmoi autōn ekratounto) — "their eyes were held/restrained." The passive voice implies divine agency — God prevents recognition so that the encounter can unfold through Scripture before sight.
- v.18f: "Κλεοπᾶς" (Kleopas) — possibly short for Cleopatros. His identity is debated; some identify him with Clopas (John 19:25). Only Luke names him. The unnamed companion has been endlessly speculated about — possibly his wife, possibly Luke himself.
- v.21g: "ἡμεῖς δὲ ἠλπίζομεν" (hēmeis de ēlpizomen) — "but we had been hoping." The imperfect tense expresses a hope that has been dashed. This is the sound of shattered expectation, one of the most emotionally honest statements in the Gospels.
- v.26h: "τὸν Χριστόν" (ton Christon) — "the Messiah." Jesus reframes suffering not as the failure of messiahship but as its very essence. "Wasn't it necessary?" (ἔδει, edei — divine necessity) challenges their assumptions about what the Messiah would do.
- v.27i: "διερμήνευσεν" (diermēneusen) — "he interpreted/explained thoroughly." The verb implies systematic, comprehensive exposition. This is the original "Bible study," with the risen Christ as teacher — and Luke tantalizingly doesn't record the specific passages.
- v.31j: "ἄφαντος ἐγένετο" (aphantos egeneto) — "he became invisible/vanished." The risen body operates differently from an ordinary body — it can appear and disappear. The recognition comes precisely at the breaking of bread, connecting to the Last Supper and to the church's ongoing communion practice.
- v.34k: An appearance to Simon Peter is mentioned here and in 1 Corinthians 15:5 but is never narrated in detail in any Gospel. It suggests a private encounter of restoration between Jesus and the disciple who denied him.
- v.36l: "εἰρήνη ὑμῖν" (eirēnē hymin) — "peace to you." The standard Hebrew greeting (shalom), but in this context it carries the weight of reconciliation and restoration after the trauma of the crucifixion and their abandonment.
- v.37m: "πνεῦμα" (pneuma) — "spirit/ghost." The disciples' fear shows that the resurrection is not what they expected. Luke goes out of his way to establish the physicality of the risen Jesus — against any notion that the resurrection was merely spiritual.
- v.42-43n: The detail of Jesus eating fish demonstrates the physical reality of his resurrection body. This anti-docetic emphasis — proving Jesus is not a phantom — was important for Luke's audience.
- v.44o: "νόμῳ Μωϋσέως καὶ τοῖς προφήταις καὶ ψαλμοῖς" (nomō Mōyseōs kai tois prophētais kai psalmois) — the Law, the Prophets, and the Psalms. This is the threefold division of the Hebrew Bible (Torah, Nevi'im, Ketuvim — with Psalms representing the Writings). Jesus claims all of Scripture testifies about him.
- v.47p: "εἰς πάντα τὰ ἔθνη" (eis panta ta ethnē) — "to all nations." Luke's universalism reaches its climax: the gospel that began in the Jerusalem temple (1:5-23) is destined for every people on earth. This verse is the bridge between the Gospel and Acts.
- v.49q: "τὴν ἐπαγγελίαν τοῦ πατρός" (tēn epangelian tou patros) — "the promise of the Father," i.e., the Holy Spirit (Acts 1:4-5, 2:1-4). "Clothed with power" (ἐνδύσησθε δύναμιν, endysēsthe dynamin) — like putting on armor or a garment. The Spirit is not an addition but an essential equipping.
- v.51r: Some manuscripts omit "and was carried up into heaven," but the ascension is narrated more fully in Acts 1:9-11. Luke alone among the Gospel writers records the ascension.
- v.53s: The Gospel ends where it began — in the temple (1:5-23). But now the disciples are there not as observers but as worshipers filled with joy, awaiting the promised power. The circle is complete, and the story of Acts is ready to begin.
