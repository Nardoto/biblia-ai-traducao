# Luke 13

**1** Right about that time, some people came and told Jesus about the Galileans whose blood Pilate had mixed with their sacrifices.ᵃ **2** He responded, "Do you think those Galileans were worse sinners than all other Galileans, because they suffered this way? **3** Absolutely not! But I'm telling you, unless you repent, you'll all perish in the same way. **4** Or those eighteen people who died when the tower at Siloam fell on them — do you think they were more guilty than everyone else living in Jerusalem? **5** Absolutely not! But I'm telling you, unless you repent, you'll all perish in the same way."

**6** Then he told this parable: "A man had a fig tree planted in his vineyard. He came looking for fruit on it but found none. **7** So he said to the gardener, 'Look — for three years now I've been coming to look for fruit on this fig tree and haven't found any. Cut it down! Why should it keep using up the soil?'

**8** "The gardener replied, 'Sir, leave it alone for one more year. I'll dig around it and fertilize it. **9** If it bears fruit next year, fine. If not — then cut it down.'"ᵇ

**10** Jesus was teaching in one of the synagogues on the Sabbath. **11** A woman was there who had been disabled by a spiritᶜ for eighteen years. She was bent over and completely unable to straighten up. **12** When Jesus saw her, he called her over and said, "Woman, you are set free from your disability." **13** He placed his hands on her, and immediately she straightened up and began praising God.

**14** But the synagogue leader, indignant that Jesus had healed on the Sabbath, said to the crowd, "There are six days for work. Come and be healed on those days — not on the Sabbath."

**15** The Lord answered him, "You hypocrites! Doesn't each of you untie your ox or donkey from the stall on the Sabbath and lead it out to water? **16** So shouldn't this woman — a daughter of Abraham — whom Satan has kept bound for eighteen long years, be set free from this bondage on the Sabbath day?"

**17** When he said this, all his opponents were humiliated. But the entire crowd rejoiced at all the wonderful things he was doing.

**18** Then he said, "What is God's kingdom like? What can I compare it to? **19** It's like a mustard seed that a man took and planted in his garden. It grew and became a tree, and the birds of the sky made nests in its branches."

**20** Again he said, "What can I compare God's kingdom to? **21** It's like yeast that a woman took and mixed into sixty poundsᵈ of flour until it worked all the way through."

**22** He traveled through towns and villages, teaching as he made his way toward Jerusalem. **23** Someone asked him, "Lord, are only a few people going to be saved?"

He said to them, **24** "Make every effort to enter through the narrow door, because many, I'm telling you, will try to enter and won't be able to. **25** Once the owner of the house gets up and shuts the door, you'll stand outside knocking, saying, 'Lord, open up for us!' But he'll answer, 'I don't know you or where you're from.' **26** Then you'll start saying, 'We ate and drank with you, and you taught in our streets!' **27** But he'll say, 'I'm telling you, I don't know where you're from. Get away from me, all of you who do wrong!'

**28** "That's where the weeping and grinding of teeth will be — when you see Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and all the prophets in God's kingdom, but you yourselves thrown out. **29** People will come from east and west, from north and south, and take their places at the feast in God's kingdom.ᵉ **30** And there are those who are last who will be first, and those who are first who will be last."

**31** Just then, some Pharisees came and told him, "Get out of here — leave! Herod wants to kill you."

**32** He replied, "Go tell that foxᶠ: 'Look, I'm driving out demons and performing healings today and tomorrow, and on the third day I reach my goal.'ᵍ **33** But I must keep going today, tomorrow, and the next day — because it's unthinkable for a prophet to die outside of Jerusalem!

**34** "Jerusalem, Jerusalem — you who kill the prophets and stone those sent to you! How often I've wanted to gather your children together, the way a hen gathers her chicks under her wings, but you weren't willing!ʰ **35** Look — your house is left to you abandoned. I'm telling you, you won't see me until you say, 'Blessed is the one who comes in the name of the Lord.'"

## Notes

- v.1a: This incident is not recorded elsewhere but fits what is known of Pilate's brutal administration. Mixing Galilean blood with their sacrifices in the temple would have been an unspeakable atrocity.
- v.6-9b: The parable of the barren fig tree — unique to Luke — balances judgment (three years, cut it down) with patience and hope (one more year, with extra care). This tension between urgency and mercy is characteristically Lukan.
- v.11c: "πνεῦμα ἀσθενείας" (pneuma astheneias) — "a spirit of infirmity/weakness." Luke attributes the condition to a spiritual cause while also recognizing its physical reality (v.16: "whom Satan has bound"). The healing story, unique to Luke, features a woman — consistent with Luke's attention to women.
- v.21d: "σάτα τρία" (sata tria) — three seahs, about 40-60 pounds of flour (roughly 22 liters). An enormous amount — enough bread for over 100 people. The extravagance of the image suggests the expansive, penetrating nature of God's kingdom.
- v.29e: A vision of the messianic banquet with people streaming in from every direction — Gentiles included. This universalism is central to Luke's theology.
- v.32f: "ἀλώπεκι" (alōpeki) — "that fox." Calling Herod a fox was a sharp insult, suggesting craftiness, insignificance, and destructiveness. Only in Luke does Jesus directly address Herod with such blunt language.
- v.32g: "τελειοῦμαι" (teleioumai) — "I am perfected/completed." The word carries overtones of reaching a destined goal or being made complete through suffering (cf. Hebrews 2:10). The "third day" foreshadows the resurrection.
- v.34h: The image of a mother hen is one of the most tender in all of Scripture — God's protective, nurturing love expressed through a humble, vulnerable image. This lament is found also in Matthew 23:37, but Luke places it in a different narrative context, during the journey to Jerusalem.
