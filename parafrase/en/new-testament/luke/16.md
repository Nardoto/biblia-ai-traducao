# Luke 16

**1** Jesus also said to his disciples, "There was a rich man who had a manager, and charges were brought to him that this manager was wasting his possessions. **2** So he called him in and said, 'What's this I hear about you? Hand over the accounts of your management, because you can't be manager any longer.'

**3** "The manager said to himself, 'What am I going to do? My master is firing me. I'm not strong enough to dig, and I'm too ashamed to beg. **4** I know what I'll do — so that when I'm removed from my position, people will welcome me into their homes.'

**5** "So he called in each of his master's debtors one by one. He asked the first, 'How much do you owe my master?'

**6** "'Eight hundred gallons of olive oil,' he said. The manager told him, 'Take your bill, sit down quickly, and write four hundred.'ᵃ

**7** "Then he asked another, 'And how much do you owe?' 'A thousand bushels of wheat,' he said. The manager told him, 'Take your bill and write eight hundred.'

**8** "And the master commended the dishonest managerᵇ for acting shrewdly. Because the people of this world are more shrewd in dealing with their own generation than the people of the light. **9** And I'm telling you: use worldly wealth to make friends for yourselves, so that when it runs out, they'll welcome you into eternal dwellings.ᶜ

**10** "Whoever is faithful with very little is also faithful with much. And whoever is dishonest with very little will be dishonest with much too. **11** So if you haven't been trustworthy with worldly wealth, who's going to trust you with true riches? **12** And if you haven't been trustworthy with someone else's property, who will give you property of your own?

**13** "No servant can serve two masters. Either they'll hate one and love the other, or they'll be devoted to one and despise the other. You can't serve both God and money."ᵈ

**14** The Pharisees, who loved money, heard all this and were sneering at him. **15** He said to them, "You're the ones who justify yourselves in front of people, but God knows your hearts. What people value highly is detestable in God's sight.

**16** "The Law and the Prophets were proclaimed until John. Since then, the good news of God's kingdom is being announced, and everyone is pressing to get in.ᵉ **17** But it's easier for heaven and earth to pass away than for one stroke of the law to drop out.

**18** "Anyone who divorces his wife and marries another woman commits adultery, and the man who marries a divorced woman commits adultery.

**19** "There was a rich man who dressed in purple and fine linen and lived in luxury every day.ᶠ **20** And at his gate lay a poor man named Lazarusᵍ — covered with sores. **21** He longed to eat what fell from the rich man's table. Even the dogs would come and lick his sores.

**22** "The poor man died and was carried by the angels to Abraham's side.ʰ The rich man also died and was buried. **23** In Hades,ⁱ where he was in torment, he looked up and saw Abraham far away, with Lazarus at his side. **24** So he called out, 'Father Abraham, have mercy on me! Send Lazarus to dip the tip of his finger in water and cool my tongue, because I'm in agony in this flame.'

**25** "Abraham replied, 'Child, remember that during your lifetime you received your good things, while Lazarus received bad things. But now he is comforted here, and you are in agony. **26** And besides all this, a great chasm has been fixed between us and you, so that those who want to cross from here to you cannot, and no one can cross from there to us.'

**27** "'Then I beg you, father, send Lazarus to my father's house — **28** I have five brothers — let him warn them, so they won't also end up in this place of torment.'

**29** "Abraham said, 'They have Moses and the Prophets. Let them listen to them.'

**30** "'No, father Abraham,' he said. 'But if someone goes to them from the dead, they'll repent.'

**31** "Abraham told him, 'If they won't listen to Moses and the Prophets, they won't be persuaded even if someone rises from the dead.'"ʲ

## Notes

- v.6a: "βάτους" (batous) — a bath, a Hebrew liquid measure of about 8-9 gallons. A hundred baths of oil would be a massive debt. The manager reduces it by 50%, then the wheat debt by 20% — creating social obligations that will benefit him later.
- v.8b: This is one of the most debated parables. The "master" (kyrios) who commends may be the rich man in the story or Jesus himself. The commendation is for shrewdness, not dishonesty. The manager's only admirable quality is his strategic use of the present to secure his future.
- v.9c: "μαμωνᾶ τῆς ἀδικίας" (mamōna tēs adikias) — "mammon of unrighteousness." Mammon is an Aramaic word for wealth/property. The genitive "of unrighteousness" characterizes worldly wealth as inherently tainted, yet it can be used redemptively through generosity.
- v.13d: "μαμωνᾷ" (mamōna) — Mammon is personified here as a rival deity demanding loyalty. The word choice elevates the warning from practical advice to a spiritual crisis: wealth can become an idol.
- v.16e: "πᾶς εἰς αὐτὴν βιάζεται" (pas eis autēn biazetai) — "everyone is pressing/forcing their way into it." The verb can be read positively (everyone is urgently pressing in) or negatively (everyone is forcing/doing violence to it). The context suggests eager, urgent pursuit.
- v.19f: Purple dye was extraordinarily expensive, made from murex snails. Fine linen (βύσσος, byssos) was Egyptian linen of the highest quality. The description paints a life of extreme opulence.
- v.20g: "Λάζαρος" (Lazaros) — the Greek form of the Hebrew "Eleazar," meaning "God helps." This is the only character in any of Jesus's parables who is given a name — the nameless rich man and the named beggar create a pointed reversal.
- v.22h: "κόλπον Ἀβραάμ" (kolpon Abraam) — "Abraham's bosom/side." A Jewish expression for the place of honor at the heavenly banquet, reclining next to Abraham. The beggar who couldn't get table scraps now feasts at the ultimate table.
- v.23i: "ᾅδης" (Hadēs) — the Greek underworld, used here for the realm of the dead. It is distinct from Gehenna. The rich man's torment and Lazarus's comfort reflect an intermediate state before final judgment.
- v.31j: The dramatic irony is stunning: Luke's readers know that someone named Lazarus will indeed be raised from the dead (John 11), and that Jesus himself will rise — and many will still not believe. The parable's final word is a sobering commentary on the sufficiency of Scripture and the stubbornness of unbelief.
