# Luke 23

**1** Then the whole assembly rose and led Jesus to Pilate. **2** They began accusing him: "We found this man subverting our nation. He opposes paying taxes to Caesar and claims to be the Messiah, a king."ᵃ

**3** Pilate asked him, "Are you the king of the Jews?"

Jesus answered, "You say so."ᵇ

**4** Then Pilate said to the chief priests and the crowds, "I find no basis for a charge against this man."

**5** But they insisted, "He stirs up the people with his teaching all over Judea — starting from Galilee and coming all the way here."

**6** When Pilate heard this, he asked if the man was a Galilean. **7** Learning that Jesus fell under Herod's jurisdiction, he sent him to Herod, who was also in Jerusalem at that time.ᶜ

**8** When Herod saw Jesus, he was extremely pleased — he'd been wanting to see him for a long time. He'd heard about him and was hoping to see him perform some kind of miracle. **9** He plied Jesus with question after question, but Jesus gave him no answer at all.ᵈ **10** The chief priests and scribes stood there, accusing him vehemently. **11** Then Herod and his soldiers treated him with contempt and mockery. They dressed him in an elegant robe and sent him back to Pilate. **12** That very day, Herod and Pilate became friends — before this, they had been enemies.ᵉ

**13** Pilate called together the chief priests, the rulers, and the people. **14** He said to them, "You brought me this man as one who was inciting the people to rebellion. I've examined him in your presence and found no basis for your charges against him. **15** Neither has Herod — that's why he sent him back to us. He's done nothing to deserve death. **16** So I'll have him flogged and release him."ᶠ

**18** But they all shouted together, "Away with this man! Release Barabbas to us!" **19** (Barabbas had been thrown into prison for an insurrection that took place in the city, and for murder.)

**20** Pilate addressed them again, wanting to release Jesus. **21** But they kept shouting, "Crucify him! Crucify him!"

**22** A third time he said to them, "Why? What crime has this man committed? I've found no grounds for a death sentence. So I'll have him flogged and release him."

**23** But they kept pressing him with loud shouts, demanding that he be crucified — and their shouts won out.ᵍ **24** Pilate decided to grant their demand. **25** He released the man who had been thrown into prison for insurrection and murder — the one they'd asked for — and handed Jesus over to their will.

**26** As they led him away, they seized Simon of Cyrene, who was coming in from the countryside, and put the cross on him to carry behind Jesus.ʰ

**27** A large crowd of people followed him, including women who were mourning and wailing for him. **28** Jesus turned to them and said, "Daughters of Jerusalem, don't weep for me. Weep for yourselves and for your children. **29** Because the days are coming when people will say, 'How blessed are the women who never bore children, the wombs that never gave birth, and the breasts that never nursed!' **30** Then they'll say to the mountains, 'Fall on us!' and to the hills, 'Cover us!'ⁱ **31** Because if people do these things when the wood is green, what will happen when it's dry?"ʲ

**32** Two other men — both criminals — were also led out to be executed with him. **33** When they arrived at the place called The Skull,ᵏ they crucified him there, along with the criminals — one on his right and one on his left.

**34** Jesus said, "Father, forgive them — they don't know what they're doing."ˡ And they divided up his clothes by casting lots.

**35** The people stood watching. The rulers even sneered at him, saying, "He saved others — let him save himself, if he's God's Messiah, the Chosen One!" **36** The soldiers also mocked him, coming up and offering him sour wine. **37** They said, "If you're the king of the Jews, save yourself!" **38** An inscription was posted above him: THIS IS THE KING OF THE JEWS.

**39** One of the criminals hanging there hurled insults at him: "Aren't you the Messiah? Save yourself and us!"

**40** But the other rebuked him: "Don't you even fear God, since you're under the same sentence? **41** We're getting what we deserve for what we did. But this man has done nothing wrong." **42** Then he said, "Jesus, remember me when you come into your kingdom."ᵐ

**43** Jesus said to him, "I'm telling you the truth — today you'll be with me in paradise."ⁿ

**44** It was now about noon, and darkness came over the whole land until three in the afternoon, **45** because the sun's light failed.ᵒ The curtain of the temple was torn in two.ᵖ

**46** Jesus cried out in a loud voice, "Father, into your hands I commit my spirit!"ᑫ And with those words, he breathed his last.

**47** When the centurion saw what had happened, he praised God and said, "This man was certainly righteous."ʳ

**48** When all the crowds who had gathered for the spectacle saw what took place, they went home beating their chests.ˢ **49** But all those who knew him, including the women who had followed him from Galilee, stood at a distance, watching these things.

**50** Now there was a man named Joseph, a member of the council — a good and righteous man. **51** He had not consented to their decision and action. He was from the Judean town of Arimathea, and he was waiting for God's kingdom. **52** He went to Pilate and asked for Jesus's body. **53** He took it down, wrapped it in linen cloth, and placed it in a tomb cut in rock — one in which no one had ever been laid. **54** It was Preparation Day, and the Sabbath was about to begin.ᵗ

**55** The women who had come with Jesus from Galilee followed along and saw the tomb and how his body was placed in it. **56** Then they went home and prepared spices and perfumes. But on the Sabbath, they rested according to the commandment.

## Notes

- v.2a: The accusations before Pilate are political, not religious — "subverting our nation," "opposes paying taxes," "claims to be a king." The charge before the Sanhedrin was blasphemy; before the Roman governor, it becomes sedition.
- v.3b: "σὺ λέγεις" (sy legeis) — "you say so." The same formula as 22:70 — affirmative but deflecting, refusing to accept the political framing of the question.
- v.7c: The Herod trial is unique to Luke. It fulfills the curious note in 9:9 that Herod wanted to see Jesus. Luke may have had information from Joanna (8:3), whose husband worked in Herod's court.
- v.9d: Jesus's silence before Herod echoes Isaiah 53:7, the silent Suffering Servant. He spoke to Pilate, the religious leaders, the women of Jerusalem — but refuses to entertain Herod's curiosity.
- v.12e: A bitter irony unique to Luke: the enemies of Jesus find common ground through their shared complicity in his condemnation.
- v.16f: Verse 17 ("Now he was obligated to release one prisoner to them at the festival") is absent from the best manuscripts and appears to have been added from Mark 15:6.
- v.23g: "κατίσχυον αἱ φωναὶ αὐτῶν" (katischyon hai phōnai autōn) — "their voices prevailed." Luke's phrasing emphasizes that mob pressure, not evidence, determined the outcome. Pilate's threefold declaration of innocence (v.4, 14, 22) is uniquely emphatic in Luke.
- v.26h: "ὄπισθεν τοῦ Ἰησοῦ" (opisthen tou Iēsou) — "behind Jesus." Simon carries the cross "behind" Jesus — language that echoes Jesus's call to discipleship: "take up your cross daily and follow me" (9:23). Simon becomes a literal image of cross-bearing discipleship.
- v.30i: Quoting Hosea 10:8 — a cry of despair for annihilation as preferable to judgment. Jesus uses it to warn of Jerusalem's coming destruction.
- v.31j: "ξύλῳ ὑγρῷ... ξηρῷ" (xylō hygrō... xērō) — a proverbial saying. If this is what Rome does to an innocent man (green wood, which doesn't burn easily), what will it do to a guilty nation (dry wood, which burns readily)?
- v.33k: "Κρανίον" (Kranion) — "the Skull," the Greek equivalent of the Aramaic "Golgotha." Luke uses only the Greek term.
- v.34l: "πάτερ, ἄφες αὐτοῖς· οὐ γὰρ οἴδασιν τί ποιοῦσιν" — This prayer of forgiveness, found only in Luke, is absent from some early manuscripts but is profoundly consistent with Luke's theology of mercy and with Jesus's own teaching (6:27-28). Many scholars consider it authentic.
- v.42m: The penitent criminal's request is extraordinary — he recognizes Jesus's kingship at the very moment of Jesus's apparent defeat. His simple request, "remember me," echoes Old Testament pleas (Psalm 106:4).
- v.43n: "παραδείσῳ" (paradeisō) — paradise, from a Persian word for an enclosed garden. In Jewish thought, it was the abode of the righteous dead. Jesus promises not just future resurrection but immediate communion: "with me."
- v.45o: "τοῦ ἡλίου ἐκλιπόντος" (tou hēliou eklipontos) — the sun's light "failing/being eclipsed." Since Passover occurs at full moon, a natural solar eclipse was impossible; Luke describes a supernatural darkness.
- v.45p: "ἐσχίσθη τὸ καταπέτασμα" (eschisthē to katapetasma) — the temple curtain was torn. Luke places this before Jesus's death (unlike Mark), making it a divine sign accompanying the crucifixion rather than a consequence of it.
- v.46q: Psalm 31:5. Jesus's last words in Luke are a prayer of trust — strikingly different from Mark's "My God, why have you forsaken me?" Luke presents a Jesus who dies in serene confidence, entrusting himself to his Father.
- v.47r: "δίκαιος" (dikaios) — "righteous/innocent." Mark has the centurion say "Son of God"; Luke has "righteous" — a more juridical and Lukan term, vindicating Jesus's innocence one final time.
- v.48s: "τυπτόμενοι τὰ στήθη" (typtomenoi ta stēthē) — beating their chests in grief and remorse. This collective mourning is unique to Luke and suggests that even the hostile crowd is moved. It foreshadows the mass repentance at Pentecost (Acts 2:37).
- v.54t: "ἐπέφωσκεν" (epephōsken) — literally "was dawning." The Sabbath begins at sunset, but the verb means "to grow light." This may reflect the Jewish practice of lighting lamps at the start of Sabbath.
