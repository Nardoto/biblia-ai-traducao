# Luke 10

**1** After this, the Lord appointed seventy-two othersᵃ and sent them out ahead of him in pairs to every town and place he was about to visit. **2** He told them, "The harvest is huge, but the workers are few. So ask the Lord of the harvest to send out workers into his harvest field.

**3** "Go! I'm sending you out like lambs among wolves. **4** Don't carry a money bag, or a travel bag, or sandals. And don't stop to greet anyone along the road.ᵇ

**5** "When you enter a house, first say, 'Peace to this house.' **6** If a person of peaceᶜ is there, your peace will rest on them. If not, it will return to you. **7** Stay in that house, eating and drinking whatever they give you, because the worker deserves their pay. Don't keep moving from house to house.

**8** "When you enter a town and they welcome you, eat what's set before you. **9** Heal the sick who are there and tell them, 'God's kingdom has come near to you.' **10** But when you enter a town and they don't welcome you, go into its streets and say, **11** 'Even the dust of your town that clings to our feet, we wipe off against you. But know this: God's kingdom has come near.' **12** I'm telling you, on that day it will be more bearable for Sodom than for that town.

**13** "What sorrow awaits you, Chorazin! What sorrow awaits you, Bethsaida! If the miracles that were done in you had been done in Tyre and Sidon, they would have repented long ago, sitting in sackcloth and ashes. **14** But it will be more bearable for Tyre and Sidon at the judgment than for you. **15** And you, Capernaum — will you be exalted to heaven? You'll be brought down to the realm of the dead!ᵈ

**16** "Whoever listens to you listens to me. Whoever rejects you rejects me. And whoever rejects me rejects the one who sent me."

**17** The seventy-two came back overjoyed. "Lord," they said, "even the demons submit to us in your name!"

**18** He replied, "I watched Satan fall from heaven like a flash of lightning.ᵉ **19** Look — I've given you authority to trample on snakes and scorpions and to overcome all the power of the enemy. Nothing will harm you. **20** But don't rejoice that the spirits submit to you. Rejoice that your names are written in heaven."

**21** In that same moment, Jesus was filled with joy through the Holy Spiritᶠ and said, "I praise you, Father, Lord of heaven and earth, because you've hidden these things from the wise and educated and revealed them to little children. Yes, Father, because this was your good pleasure."

**22** Then he said, "Everything has been entrusted to me by my Father. No one knows who the Son is except the Father, and no one knows who the Father is except the Son — and anyone the Son chooses to reveal him to."ᵍ

**23** Turning to his disciples privately, he said, "How blessed are the eyes that see what you're seeing. **24** I'm telling you, many prophets and kings wanted to see what you see but didn't see it, and to hear what you hear but didn't hear it."

**25** One day, an expert in the lawʰ stood up to test Jesus. "Teacher," he asked, "what must I do to inherit eternal life?"

**26** Jesus said, "What is written in the law? How do you read it?"

**27** He answered, "'Love the Lord your God with all your heart, with all your soul, with all your strength, and with all your mind,' and 'Love your neighbor as yourself.'"

**28** "You've answered correctly," Jesus said. "Do this, and you'll live."

**29** But wanting to justify himself, the man asked Jesus, "And who is my neighbor?"

**30** Jesus replied with a story: "A man was going down from Jerusalem to Jericho when he fell into the hands of robbers. They stripped him, beat him, and left him half dead. **31** By chance, a priest was going down that same road. When he saw the man, he passed by on the other side. **32** A Levite came along too, and when he saw him, he also passed by on the other side. **33** But a Samaritan,ⁱ who was traveling that way, came upon him and was moved with compassion.ʲ **34** He went to him, bandaged his wounds, pouring on oil and wine. Then he put the man on his own animal, brought him to an inn, and took care of him. **35** The next day, he took out two denariiᵏ and gave them to the innkeeper. 'Take care of him,' he said, 'and whatever extra you spend, I'll reimburse you on my way back.'

**36** "Which of these three do you think proved to be a neighbor to the man who fell into the robbers' hands?"

**37** The expert said, "The one who showed him mercy."

Jesus told him, "Go and do the same."

**38** As they continued their journey, Jesus entered a village where a woman named Martha welcomed him into her home. **39** She had a sister named Mary, who sat at the Lord's feet and was listening to what he said.ˡ **40** But Martha was overwhelmed with all the preparations. She came to him and said, "Lord, don't you care that my sister has left me to do all the work by myself? Tell her to help me!"

**41** The Lord answered her, "Martha, Martha, you're worried and upset about so many things. **42** But only one thing is necessary.ᵐ Mary has chosen the better part, and it won't be taken from her."

## Notes

- v.1a: Manuscripts differ between "seventy" and "seventy-two." The number likely alludes to the seventy nations of Genesis 10 (LXX has seventy-two), suggesting a universal mission beyond Israel. This sending is unique to Luke.
- v.4b: "μηδένα κατὰ τὴν ὁδὸν ἀσπάσησθε" — not rudeness but urgency. Eastern greetings were lengthy and elaborate; the mission can't wait.
- v.6c: "υἱὸς εἰρήνης" (huios eirēnēs) — literally "a son of peace," a Semitic idiom for someone who is receptive to the message.
- v.15d: "ἕως ᾅδου καταβήσῃ" (heōs hadou katabēsē) — "you'll go down to Hades." An echo of Isaiah 14:13-15, the fall of the king of Babylon. Privilege brings responsibility.
- v.18e: "ἐθεώρουν τὸν Σατανᾶν... πεσόντα" (etheōroun ton Satanan... pesonta) — "I was watching Satan fall." The imperfect tense (was watching) with the aorist participle (having fallen) creates a vivid image. Whether this refers to a primordial fall, the disciples' present victories, or a prophetic vision is deliberately ambiguous.
- v.21f: "ἠγαλλιάσατο ἐν τῷ πνεύματι τῷ ἁγίῳ" — Jesus "rejoiced in the Holy Spirit." This is the only place in the Gospels where Jesus is explicitly described as experiencing joy. Uniquely Lukan.
- v.22g: This verse has been called a "Johannine thunderbolt" because its language about mutual knowledge between Father and Son resembles John's Gospel more than the Synoptics.
- v.25h: "νομικός" (nomikos) — a legal expert, distinct from a "scribe" (grammateus). Luke uses this term, more familiar to Greek-speaking readers.
- v.33i: The Samaritan hero would have been deeply shocking to a Jewish audience. Samaritans were despised as religious and ethnic outsiders. Jesus deliberately makes the "enemy" the hero of the story.
- v.33j: "ἐσπλαγχνίσθη" (esplanchnisthē) — the same visceral compassion word used of Jesus in 7:13. The Samaritan mirrors the divine compassion.
- v.35k: Two denarii would cover about two days' wages — enough for an extended stay at the inn.
- v.39l: Mary sitting "at the Lord's feet" is the posture of a disciple learning from a rabbi — an extraordinary role for a woman in first-century culture. Luke consistently elevates women's dignity.
- v.42m: "ἑνὸς δέ ἐστιν χρεία" — "one thing is necessary." Some manuscripts read "few things are necessary, or only one." The point remains: attentiveness to Jesus takes priority.
