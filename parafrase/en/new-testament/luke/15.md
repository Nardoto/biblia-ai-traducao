# Luke 15

**1** Now all the tax collectors and sinners were gathering around to listen to Jesus. **2** The Pharisees and the scribes were grumbling: "This man welcomes sinners and eats with them."ᵃ

**3** So he told them this parable:

**4** "Which one of you, if you have a hundred sheep and lose one of them, doesn't leave the ninety-nine in the open country and go after the lost one until you find it? **5** And when you find it, you joyfully put it on your shoulders **6** and go home. Then you call your friends and neighbors together and say, 'Celebrate with me! I found my lost sheep!' **7** I'm telling you, in the same way there will be more joy in heaven over one sinner who repents than over ninety-nine righteous people who don't need to repent.ᵇ

**8** "Or what woman who has ten silver coinsᶜ and loses one of them doesn't light a lamp, sweep the house, and search carefully until she finds it? **9** And when she finds it, she calls her friends and neighbors together and says, 'Celebrate with me! I found the coin I lost!' **10** In the same way, I'm telling you, there's joy in the presence of God's angels over one sinner who repents."

**11** Then he said, "A man had two sons. **12** The younger one said to his father, 'Father, give me my share of the estate.'ᵈ So the father divided his property between them.

**13** "Not many days later, the younger son gathered everything he had and traveled to a distant country, where he squandered his wealth in reckless living. **14** After he'd spent everything, a severe famine hit that whole country, and he found himself in desperate need. **15** So he went and hired himself out to one of the citizens of that country, who sent him into his fields to feed pigs.ᵉ **16** He longed to fill his stomach with the pods the pigs were eating, but no one gave him anything.

**17** "When he came to his senses,ᶠ he said, 'How many of my father's hired workers have more than enough food, and here I am dying of hunger! **18** I'll get up and go to my father and say to him: Father, I've sinned against heaven and against you. **19** I don't deserve to be called your son anymore. Treat me like one of your hired workers.' **20** So he got up and went to his father.

"But while he was still a long way off, his father saw him and was filled with compassion.ᵍ He ran to his son, threw his arms around him, and kissed him over and over.

**21** "The son said to him, 'Father, I've sinned against heaven and against you. I don't deserve to be called your son anymore.'

**22** "But the father said to his servants, 'Quick! Bring out the best robe and put it on him. Put a ring on his finger and sandals on his feet.ʰ **23** Bring the fattened calf and slaughter it. Let's eat and celebrate! **24** Because this son of mine was dead and has come back to life — he was lost and has been found!' And they began to celebrate.

**25** "Meanwhile, the older son was out in the field. As he came near the house, he heard music and dancing. **26** He called one of the servants over and asked what was going on. **27** The servant told him, 'Your brother has come home, and your father has slaughtered the fattened calf because he got him back safe and sound.'

**28** "The older brother became furious and refused to go in. So his father came out and pleaded with him. **29** But he answered his father, 'Look! All these years I've slaved for you. I never disobeyed a single command of yours. And you never even gave me a young goat so I could celebrate with my friends. **30** But when this son of yoursⁱ comes home — the one who devoured your wealth with prostitutes — you slaughter the fattened calf for him!'

**31** "'My son,'ʲ the father said, 'you're always with me, and everything I have is yours. **32** But we had to celebrate and be glad, because this brother of yours was dead and is alive again — he was lost and has been found.'"

## Notes

- v.2a: "προσδέχεται... καὶ συνεσθίει αὐτοῖς" (prosdechetai... kai synesthiei autois) — "welcomes them and eats with them." Table fellowship in the ancient world signified acceptance and solidarity. The Pharisees' complaint sets the stage for all three parables in this chapter.
- v.7b: The phrase "ninety-nine righteous persons who do not need repentance" may carry gentle irony — are there really such people? Or is Jesus describing how the Pharisees see themselves?
- v.8c: "δραχμήν" (drachmēn) — a drachma, a Greek silver coin roughly equivalent to a denarius (a day's wage). The ten coins may represent a woman's dowry or her entire savings, making even one coin's loss significant.
- v.12d: Requesting one's inheritance while the father is still alive was a profound insult — essentially saying, "I wish you were dead." The father's compliance is the first shocking act of grace in the story.
- v.15e: For a Jewish person, feeding pigs — unclean animals — represented the absolute lowest point, total alienation from one's identity and community.
- v.17f: "εἰς ἑαυτὸν ἐλθών" (eis heauton elthōn) — "coming to himself." A beautiful Greek idiom suggesting that in his degradation he had been outside himself, alienated from his true self. Repentance is a kind of homecoming even before the physical return.
- v.20g: "ἐσπλαγχνίσθη" (esplanchnisthē) — the same gut-level compassion word used of Jesus (7:13) and the Good Samaritan (10:33). The father's response mirrors the divine compassion. A Middle Eastern patriarch running was undignified — he would have to hike up his robes. He sacrifices his dignity for love.
- v.22h: The robe signifies honor restored, the ring signifies authority (a signet ring for family business), and the sandals signify status as a son (slaves went barefoot). Every detail reverses the son's self-imposed degradation.
- v.30i: "ὁ υἱός σου οὗτος" (ho huios sou houtos) — "this son of yours." The older brother refuses to say "my brother," distancing himself from both the prodigal and, subtly, from the father who welcomes him.
- v.31j: "τέκνον" (teknon) — "child," an intimate, tender form of address. The father responds to the older son's anger with warmth, not rebuke.
