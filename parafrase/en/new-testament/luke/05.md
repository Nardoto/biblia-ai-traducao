# Luke 5

**1** One day, as the crowd was pressing in on Jesus to hear the word of God, he was standing by the Lake of Gennesaret.ᵃ **2** He noticed two boats at the water's edge — the fishermen had gotten out and were washing their nets. **3** He got into one of the boats, the one that belonged to Simon, and asked him to push out a little from shore. Then he sat down and taught the crowds from the boat.

**4** When he'd finished speaking, he said to Simon, "Head out into the deep water and let down your nets for a catch."

**5** Simon replied, "Master,ᵇ we worked hard all night and caught nothing. But because you say so, I'll let down the nets."

**6** When they did, they hauled in such an enormous number of fish that their nets started tearing. **7** They signaled to their partners in the other boat to come help. They came, and they filled both boats so full that they began to sink.

**8** When Simon Peter saw this, he fell at Jesus's knees and said, "Go away from me, Lord — I'm a sinful man!" **9** Because he and everyone with him were completely stunned by the catch they'd taken in — **10** and so were James and John, Zebedee's sons, who were Simon's partners. Jesus said to Simon, "Don't be afraid. From now on, you'll be catching people."ᶜ

**11** After they'd brought the boats to shore, they left everything behind and followed him.

**12** While Jesus was in one of the towns, a man covered with leprosyᵈ came along. When he saw Jesus, he fell facedown and begged him, "Lord, if you're willing, you can make me clean."

**13** Jesus reached out his hand and touched him — saying, "I am willing. Be clean." And immediately the leprosy left him.

**14** Then Jesus instructed him, "Don't tell anyone. Go show yourself to the priest and offer the sacrifices Moses commanded for your cleansing, as evidence for them."ᵉ

**15** But the word about him spread even more, and large crowds kept gathering to hear him and to be healed of their illnesses. **16** Yet he would often withdraw to deserted places to pray.ᶠ

**17** One day, while he was teaching, some Pharisees and teachers of the law were sitting there — they'd come from every village of Galilee and Judea, and from Jerusalem. And the Lord's power was present for him to heal.

**18** Some men came carrying a paralyzed man on a mat. They tried to bring him in and set him before Jesus, **19** but they couldn't find a way through because of the crowd. So they went up on the roof, removed some tiles, and lowered him on his mat right into the middle of the room, directly in front of Jesus.ᵍ

**20** When Jesus saw their faith, he said, "Friend, your sins are forgiven."ʰ

**21** The scribes and the Pharisees began reasoning among themselves: "Who is this man speaking blasphemy? Who can forgive sins except God alone?"

**22** But Jesus knew what they were thinking and responded, "Why are you reasoning like this in your hearts? **23** Which is easier to say — 'Your sins are forgiven,' or 'Get up and walk'? **24** But so that you'll know that the Son of Manⁱ has authority on earth to forgive sins" — he turned to the paralyzed man — "I'm telling you: Get up, pick up your mat, and go home."

**25** Instantly, right in front of them, the man stood up, picked up what he'd been lying on, and went home praising God. **26** Everyone was completely amazed. They were filled with awe and said, "We've seen extraordinary things today!"

**27** After this, Jesus went out and noticed a tax collector named Levi sitting at his tax booth. Jesus said to him, "Follow me." **28** Levi got up, left everything, and followed him.

**29** Then Levi threw a great banquet for Jesus at his house. There was a large crowd of tax collectors and others reclining at the table with them. **30** But the Pharisees and their scribes complained to Jesus's disciples, "Why do you eat and drink with tax collectors and sinners?"

**31** Jesus answered them, "It's not the healthy who need a doctor — it's the sick. **32** I haven't come to call the righteous, but sinners, to repentance."ʲ

**33** They said to him, "John's disciples frequently fast and pray, and so do the disciples of the Pharisees. But yours keep eating and drinking."

**34** Jesus replied, "Can you make wedding guests fast while the groom is with them? **35** But the days are coming when the groom will be taken from them, and then — in those days — they will fast."

**36** He also told them a parable: "Nobody tears a patch from a new garment to put on an old one. If they do, they'll ruin the new garment, and the patch from the new won't match the old. **37** And nobody pours new wine into old wineskins. If they do, the new wine will burst the skins — the wine will spill out and the skins will be ruined. **38** No, new wine has to go into fresh wineskins. **39** And nobody who's been drinking aged wine wants new wine right away, because they say, 'The old is fine.'"ᵏ

## Notes

- v.1a: "λίμνην Γεννησαρέτ" (limnēn Gennēsaret) — Luke, writing for a Greco-Roman audience, uses the proper Greek term limnē (lake) rather than the Semitic "Sea of Galilee." Gennesaret is the fertile plain on the northwest shore.
- v.5b: "ἐπιστάτα" (epistata) — a term used only in Luke, meaning "master" or "chief." It's a respectful address of authority, distinct from "rabbi" or "lord."
- v.10c: "ἀνθρώπους ἔσῃ ζωγρῶν" (anthrōpous esē zōgrōn) — literally "you will be catching people alive." The verb zōgreō means to capture alive, implying rescue rather than capture for destruction.
- v.12d: "πλήρης λέπρας" (plērēs lepras) — "full of leprosy," a detail unique to Luke the physician, suggesting an advanced stage of the disease.
- v.14e: "εἰς μαρτύριον αὐτοῖς" (eis martyrion autois) — "as evidence/testimony to them." The cleansing, verified by priests, would serve as proof of Jesus's authority.
- v.16f: Luke repeatedly emphasizes Jesus's prayer life (3:21; 6:12; 9:18, 28-29; 11:1; 22:41-44). This is a distinctive Lukan theme.
- v.19g: Luke specifies "tiles" (κεράμων, keramōn) for the roof material, adapting the scene for a Greco-Roman audience familiar with tiled roofs, whereas Mark describes digging through a mud-and-thatch roof.
- v.20h: "ἄνθρωπε, ἀφέωνταί σοι αἱ ἁμαρτίαι σου" — Jesus addresses him as "anthrōpe" (person/friend), a respectful but general address. The perfect tense of "forgiven" indicates sins already and completely pardoned.
- v.24i: "υἱὸς τοῦ ἀνθρώπου" (huios tou anthrōpou) — "Son of Man," Jesus's preferred self-designation, echoing Daniel 7:13-14. The title carries both humility (human being) and apocalyptic authority.
- v.32j: Luke adds "εἰς μετάνοιαν" (eis metanoian) — "to repentance," specifying the purpose of calling sinners, absent in Mark's parallel.
- v.39k: This verse, unique to Luke, may reflect the reluctance of those comfortable with traditional religion to embrace Jesus's new message. "The old is fine" — or as some manuscripts read, "the old is better" — captures the human preference for the familiar.
