# Luke 18

**1** Then Jesus told them a parable about the need to always pray and never give up:ᵃ

**2** "In a certain town there was a judge who had no fear of God and no respect for people. **3** And in that town there was a widow who kept coming to him, saying, 'Grant me justice against my adversary!'

**4** "For a while he refused. But eventually he said to himself, 'Even though I don't fear God or respect anyone, **5** because this widow keeps bothering me, I'll see that she gets justice — so she doesn't wear me out with her constant coming!'"ᵇ

**6** The Lord said, "Listen to what the unjust judge says. **7** And won't God bring about justice for his chosen ones, who cry out to him day and night? Will he keep putting them off? **8** I'm telling you, he'll see that they get justice — and quickly! But when the Son of Man comes, will he find faith on the earth?"ᶜ

**9** He also told this parable to certain people who were confident of their own righteousness and looked down on everyone else:

**10** "Two men went up to the temple to pray. One was a Pharisee and the other a tax collector. **11** The Pharisee stood up and prayed about himself:ᵈ 'God, I thank you that I'm not like other people — thieves, wrongdoers, adulterers — or even like this tax collector. **12** I fast twice a week and give a tenth of everything I earn.'

**13** "But the tax collector stood at a distance and wouldn't even lift his eyes to heaven. He kept beating his chest and saying, 'God, have mercy on me — I'm a sinner!'ᵉ

**14** "I'm telling you, this man went home justified before God, rather than the other one. Because everyone who exalts themselves will be humbled, and whoever humbles themselves will be exalted."

**15** People were bringing even their babies to Jesus for him to touch them. When the disciples saw this, they scolded them. **16** But Jesus called the children to him and said, "Let the little children come to me — don't try to stop them! God's kingdom belongs to people like these. **17** I'm telling you the truth: whoever doesn't receive God's kingdom like a child will never enter it."

**18** A certain rulerᶠ asked him, "Good Teacher, what must I do to inherit eternal life?"

**19** Jesus said to him, "Why do you call me good? No one is good except God alone. **20** You know the commandments: 'Don't commit adultery. Don't murder. Don't steal. Don't give false testimony. Honor your father and mother.'"

**21** The man said, "I've kept all of these since I was young."

**22** When Jesus heard this, he said, "There's still one thing you're missing. Sell everything you have and give it to the poor, and you'll have treasure in heaven. Then come follow me."

**23** When the man heard this, he became deeply sad, because he was extremely wealthy.

**24** Jesus looked at him and said, "How hard it is for those who have wealth to enter God's kingdom! **25** It's easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for a rich person to enter God's kingdom."ᵍ

**26** Those who heard this said, "Then who can be saved?"

**27** He replied, "What's impossible for people is possible for God."

**28** Peter said, "Look, we've left everything we had and followed you."

**29** Jesus said to them, "I'm telling you the truth: there's no one who has left home, wife, brothers, parents, or children for the sake of God's kingdom **30** who won't receive many times as much in this present age — and in the age to come, eternal life."

**31** He took the Twelve aside and told them, "Listen — we're going up to Jerusalem, and everything the prophets wrote about the Son of Man will be fulfilled. **32** He'll be handed over to the Gentiles, mocked, insulted, and spat on. **33** After flogging him, they'll kill him. And on the third day, he'll rise again."

**34** But they understood none of this. The meaning was hidden from them, and they had no idea what he was talking about.ʰ

**35** As Jesus approached Jericho, a blind man was sitting by the roadside begging. **36** Hearing a crowd going by, he asked what was happening. **37** They told him, "Jesus of Nazareth is passing by."

**38** So he called out, "Jesus, Son of David, have mercy on me!"

**39** The people in front told him to be quiet. But he shouted all the louder, "Son of David, have mercy on me!"

**40** Jesus stopped and ordered the man to be brought to him. When he came near, Jesus asked him, **41** "What do you want me to do for you?"

"Lord, I want to see," he said.

**42** Jesus told him, "Receive your sight. Your faith has healed you."ⁱ **43** Immediately he could see, and he followed Jesus, praising God. When all the people saw it, they also praised God.

## Notes

- v.1a: "πάντοτε προσεύχεσθαι... καὶ μὴ ἐγκακεῖν" (pantote proseuchesthai... kai mē enkakein) — "to always pray and not lose heart/give up." Luke alone provides this interpretive framework before the parable.
- v.5b: "ὑπωπιάζῃ" (hypōpiazē) — literally "give me a black eye" or "strike me under the eye." A vivid, almost comic expression for persistent pestering. The humor makes the point memorable.
- v.8c: Jesus's final question — "will he find faith on the earth?" — is jarring and left unanswered. It shifts the parable from a teaching about God's reliability to a searching question about human perseverance.
- v.11d: "πρὸς ἑαυτὸν ταῦτα προσηύχετο" (pros heauton tauta prosēucheto) — this can be read as "stood by himself and prayed" or "prayed about/to himself." The ambiguity may be intentional — his prayer is more self-congratulation than genuine conversation with God.
- v.13e: "ὁ θεός, ἱλάσθητί μοι τῷ ἁμαρτωλῷ" (ho theos, hilasthēti moi tō hamartōlō) — "God, be merciful/propitious to me, the sinner." The definite article "the" (tō) is significant — he doesn't say "a sinner" but "the sinner," as if he were the worst one. The verb hilaskomai is a sacrificial term, asking for atonement.
- v.18f: "ἄρχων" (archōn) — a ruler or leader. Luke alone specifies his status, which Mark and Matthew lack. Combined with his wealth, this makes his inability to follow Jesus all the more striking.
- v.25g: The "eye of a needle" is almost certainly hyperbolic, not a reference to a supposed small gate in Jerusalem (for which there is no historical evidence). Jesus uses deliberate absurdity to make the point about impossibility — which sets up verse 27.
- v.34h: Luke emphasizes the disciples' incomprehension three different ways: they understood none of it, it was hidden from them, and they didn't know what he was talking about. The triple emphasis is unique to Luke and heightens the drama of the approaching passion.
- v.42i: "ἡ πίστις σου σέσωκέν σε" (hē pistis sou sesōken se) — the same formula as 7:50 and 17:19. Physical healing and spiritual salvation intertwine in this recurring phrase.
