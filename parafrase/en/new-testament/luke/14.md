# Luke 14

**1** One Sabbath, Jesus went to eat at the home of a prominent Pharisee, and they were watching him closely. **2** Right there in front of him was a man suffering from severe swelling.ᵃ **3** Jesus addressed the legal experts and Pharisees: "Is it lawful to heal on the Sabbath, or not?" **4** They stayed silent. So he took hold of the man, healed him, and sent him on his way.

**5** Then he said to them, "Which of you, if your son or your ox falls into a well on the Sabbath, wouldn't immediately pull them out?" **6** They had nothing to say in response.

**7** When he noticed how the guests were choosing the places of honor at the table, he told them a parable: **8** "When someone invites you to a wedding feast, don't sit down in the place of honor. Someone more distinguished than you may have been invited, **9** and the host who invited both of you might come and say, 'Give this person your seat.' Then, humiliated, you'll have to take the lowest place. **10** Instead, when you're invited, go and sit in the lowest place. That way, when your host comes, they'll say, 'Friend, move up to a better seat.' Then you'll be honored in front of everyone at the table. **11** Because everyone who exalts themselves will be humbled, and everyone who humbles themselves will be exalted."ᵇ

**12** Then he said to the one who had invited him, "When you throw a lunch or dinner, don't invite your friends, your brothers, your relatives, or your rich neighbors — because they might invite you back, and that would be your repayment. **13** Instead, when you host a banquet, invite the poor, the disabled, the lame, and the blind. **14** Then you'll be blessed, because they can't repay you. You'll be repaid at the resurrection of the righteous."ᶜ

**15** When one of the dinner guests heard this, he said to Jesus, "How blessed is the person who will eat at the feast in God's kingdom!"

**16** Jesus replied, "A man was giving a great banquet and invited many guests. **17** When the time came for the banquet, he sent his servant to tell those who had been invited, 'Come — everything is ready now.'

**18** "But they all started making excuses. The first said, 'I just bought a field and need to go inspect it. Please excuse me.' **19** Another said, 'I just bought five yoke of oxen and I'm on my way to try them out. Please excuse me.' **20** Still another said, 'I just got married, so I can't come.'

**21** "The servant came back and reported this to his master. Then the master of the house became angry and said to his servant, 'Go out quickly into the streets and alleyways of the town and bring in the poor, the disabled, the blind, and the lame.'

**22** "'Sir,' the servant said, 'what you ordered has been done, and there's still room.'

**23** "The master told the servant, 'Go out to the roads and country lanes and compel people to come in, so that my house will be full.ᵈ **24** Because I'm telling you, not one of those originally invited will taste my banquet.'"

**25** Large crowds were traveling with Jesus, and he turned and said to them, **26** "If anyone comes to me and doesn't hateᵉ their father and mother, wife and children, brothers and sisters — yes, even their own life — they can't be my disciple. **27** Whoever doesn't carry their own cross and follow me can't be my disciple.

**28** "Suppose one of you wants to build a tower. Won't you first sit down and estimate the cost, to see if you have enough to finish it? **29** Otherwise, after laying the foundation, you might not be able to complete it, and everyone watching will ridicule you, **30** saying, 'This person started building but couldn't finish!'

**31** "Or suppose a king is about to go to war against another king. Won't he first sit down and consider whether he can, with ten thousand men, stand up against the one coming against him with twenty thousand? **32** If he can't, he'll send a delegation while the other is still a long way off and ask for terms of peace.

**33** "In the same way, anyone who doesn't give up everything they have can't be my disciple.

**34** "Salt is good. But if salt loses its flavor, how can it be made salty again?ᶠ **35** It's no good for the soil or for the manure pile — it gets thrown out. Whoever has ears to hear — listen!"

## Notes

- v.2a: "ὑδρωπικός" (hydrōpikos) — suffering from dropsy/edema, an abnormal accumulation of fluid causing swelling. This medical term appears only here in the NT — fitting for Luke the physician. This healing is unique to Luke.
- v.11b: This principle of reversal — the humble exalted, the exalted humbled — is one of Luke's central theological themes, announced in Mary's Magnificat (1:52) and echoed throughout the Gospel.
- v.14c: "ἐν τῇ ἀναστάσει τῶν δικαίων" (en tē anastasei tōn dikaiōn) — "at the resurrection of the righteous." Jesus presupposes a future resurrection and connects present generosity to future reward — but the reward is God's, not human reciprocation.
- v.23d: The two waves of invitation (first to the streets, then to the roads and lanes) have often been read as extending first to outcasts within Israel, then to Gentiles. The parable of the great banquet is unique to Luke in this form.
- v.26e: "μισεῖ" (misei) — "hates." In Semitic idiom, "hate" can function as a comparative expression meaning "love less than" (cf. Genesis 29:31, where Leah is "hated," meaning less favored). Jesus is demanding that loyalty to him must surpass even the strongest family bonds — not that one should literally despise family.
- v.34f: Salt in the ancient world (often impure, from the Dead Sea) could lose its saltiness through chemical breakdown, leaving a useless mineral residue. The metaphor warns against disciples who lose their distinctive commitment.
