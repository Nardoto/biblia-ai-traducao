# Romans 4

**1** So what do we say about Abraham, our forefather according to the flesh?ᵃ What did he discover? **2** If Abraham was declared righteous by works, he'd have something to boast about — but not before God. **3** Because what does the Scripture say? "Abraham believed God, and it was credited to him as righteousness."ᵇ

**4** Now, when a person works, their wages aren't counted as a gift — they're counted as what's owed. **5** But to the person who doesn't work but simply trusts the one who declares the ungodly righteous, their faith is credited as righteousness.

**6** David says the same thing when he speaks of the blessedness of the person to whom God credits righteousness apart from works:

**7** "How blessed are those whose lawless acts have been forgiven
and whose sins have been covered over!
**8** How blessed is the person whose sin the Lord will never count against them!"ᶜ

**9** Now, is this blessedness only for the circumcised, or for the uncircumcised too? We just said that Abraham's faith was credited to him as righteousness. **10** Under what circumstances was it credited? When he was circumcised or before? Not after — before! **11** He received the sign of circumcision as a sealᵈ of the righteousness he already had by faith while still uncircumcised — so that he'd be the father of all who believe without being circumcised, so that righteousness would be credited to them too, **12** and also the father of the circumcised — not just those who are circumcised, but those who also walk in the footsteps of the faith our father Abraham had while he was still uncircumcised.

**13** The promise to Abraham and his descendants that he'd be heir of the worldᵉ didn't come through the Law but through the righteousness that comes from faith. **14** Because if those who live by the Law are the heirs, then faith has been emptied of meaning and the promise has been wiped out. **15** The Law produces wrathᶠ — because where there's no law, there's no violation either.

**16** That's why it depends on faith — so that it can be by grace, so that the promise is guaranteed to all Abraham's descendants: not only to those who have the Law but also to those who share Abraham's faith. He's the father of us all — **17** as it's written, "I have made you a father of many nations." He is our father in the sight of the God he believed in — the God who gives life to the dead and calls into existence things that don't yet exist.ᵍ

**18** Against all hope, Abraham believed in hope, so that he became the father of many nations, just as he'd been told: "So shall your descendants be." **19** Without weakening in his faith, he faced the fact that his own body was as good as deadʰ — he was about a hundred years old — and that Sarah's womb was dead too. **20** He didn't waverⁱ in unbelief regarding God's promise. Instead, he grew strong in faith, giving glory to God, **21** fully convinced that God had the power to do what he'd promised. **22** That's why "it was credited to him as righteousness."

**23** Now the words "it was credited to him" weren't written just for his benefit — **24** they were written for us too. It will be credited to us who believe in the one who raised Jesus our Lord from the dead — **25** who was handed over because of our transgressions and was raised for our justification.ʲ

## Notes

- v.1a: Some manuscripts read "What then shall we say that Abraham our forefather according to the flesh has found?" The textual tradition varies slightly, but the sense remains the same.
- v.3b: Quoted from Genesis 15:6 (LXX). *Elogisthē* (ἐλογίσθη) — "was credited/reckoned." This is accounting language — something placed to one's account. Paul uses *logizomai* eleven times in this chapter, making it the keyword of his argument.
- v.8c: Quoted from Psalm 32:1–2 (LXX 31:1–2). Paul pairs David with Abraham to show that righteousness-by-faith isn't a fringe idea but the consistent testimony of Israel's greatest figures.
- v.11d: *sphragida* (σφραγῖδα) — "seal," an authentication mark. Circumcision confirmed what was already true; it didn't create the reality.
- v.13e: *klēronomon... tou kosmou* (κληρονόμον... τοῦ κόσμου) — "heir of the world." Genesis doesn't use these exact words; Paul expands the land promise to cosmic scope, reflecting Jewish interpretive tradition that understood Abraham's inheritance as encompassing the entire renewed creation.
- v.15f: Paul's logic is compressed: the Law, by defining violations, actually amplifies transgression and brings wrath. Without law, there's still sin (cf. 5:13), but no formal violation of a stated command.
- v.17g: *kalountos ta mē onta hōs onta* (καλοῦντος τὰ μὴ ὄντα ὡς ὄντα) — "calling things that are not as though they are." God's word is performative — when God names something, it comes into being. This is the God Abraham trusted.
- v.19h: *nenekrōmenon* (νενεκρωμένον) — "having been made dead," a perfect passive participle. Abraham's body was already in a state of deadness. The resurrection theme runs underneath: the God who gives life to the dead (v.17) is the same God who made Abraham's dead body fruitful.
- v.20i: *diekrithē* (διεκρίθη) — "he did not waver/be divided." The word suggests inner division or vacillation. Abraham's faith wasn't a momentary leap but a sustained, undivided trust.
- v.25j: *dia tēn dikaiōsin hēmōn* (διὰ τὴν δικαίωσιν ἡμῶν) — "for our justification." The resurrection isn't just proof that Jesus' death worked — it's the event through which our right standing with God is actualized. Death dealt with the penalty; resurrection secured the verdict.
