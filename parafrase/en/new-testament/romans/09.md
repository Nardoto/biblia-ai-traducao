# Romans 9

**1** I'm telling the truth in the Messiah — I'm not lying, my conscience confirms it through the Holy Spirit — **2** I carry enormous grief, an unrelenting ache in my heart. **3** I could wish that I myself were cut off from the Messiahᵃ — accursed — for the sake of my own people, my flesh-and-blood relatives. **4** They're Israelites — the adoptionᵇ as God's children belongs to them, and the glory, and the covenants, and the giving of the Law, and the temple worship, and the promises. **5** The patriarchs are theirs, and from them — as far as his human descent goes — came the Messiah, who is God over all, blessed forever.ᶜ Amen.

**6** But it's not as if God's word has failed.ᵈ Because not everyone descended from Israel actually is Israel. **7** And not all of Abraham's descendants are his true children. No — "It is through Isaac that your offspring will be named."ᵉ **8** In other words, it's not the children born naturally who are God's children, but the children born through the promise who are counted as descendants. **9** Because here's what the promise said: "At the appointed time I will come, and Sarah will have a son."

**10** And that's not all. Rebecca also conceived children by one man — our father Isaac. **11** Before the twins had been born or had done anything good or bad — so that God's purpose in electionᶠ would stand, **12** based not on works but on the one who calls — she was told, "The older will serve the younger." **13** As it's written: "Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated."ᵍ

**14** So what do we say? Is God unjust? Absolutely not! **15** He says to Moses, "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion."ʰ **16** So then, it doesn't depend on human desire or effort but on God who shows mercy. **17** The Scripture says to Pharaoh: "I raised you up for this very purpose — to display my power in you and to have my name proclaimed in all the earth." **18** So then, God has mercy on whom he wants, and he hardens whom he wants.

**19** You'll say to me, "Then why does God still blame anyone? Who can resist his will?" **20** But who are you — a mere human — to talk back to God?ⁱ Will what is formed say to the one who formed it, "Why did you make me like this?" **21** Doesn't the potter have the right over the clay to make from the same lump one vessel for special use and another for ordinary use?

**22** What if God, wanting to show his wrath and make his power known, endured with great patience the objects of wrathʲ — made for destruction — **23** in order to make known the riches of his glory to the objects of mercy, which he prepared in advance for glory? **24** That's us — the ones he called, not only from the Jews but also from the Gentiles.

**25** As he says in Hosea: "Those who were not my people I will call 'my people,' and the one who was not loved I will call 'beloved.'" **26** "And in the very place where it was said to them, 'You are not my people,' there they'll be called children of the living God."

**27** Isaiah cries out about Israel: "Even if the number of Israel's children were like the sand of the sea, only the remnantᵏ will be saved. **28** Because the Lord will carry out his sentence on earth completely and decisively." **29** And as Isaiah predicted: "If the Lord of Armies hadn't left us descendants, we would've ended up like Sodom and been made like Gomorrah."

**30** So what do we say? That Gentiles who weren't pursuing righteousness obtained it — a righteousness that comes from faith — **31** but Israel, who was pursuing a law of righteousness, didn't reach that law? **32** Why? Because they pursued it not by faith but as though it could be achieved by works. They stumbled over the stumbling stone,ˡ **33** as it's written: "Look, I'm placing in Zion a stone that causes stumbling, a rock that makes people fall — and the one who believes in him will never be put to shame."

## Notes

- v.3a: *anathema* (ἀνάθεμα) — "cursed, cut off." Paul is echoing Moses' willingness to be blotted out of God's book for Israel's sake (Exodus 32:32). The depth of Paul's anguish for his people is staggering.
- v.4b: *huiothesia* (υἱοθεσία) — "adoption." Israel was God's adopted son (Exodus 4:22). Paul lists Israel's privileges in a crescendo: adoption, glory, covenants, Law, worship, promises, patriarchs, and the Messiah himself.
- v.5c: *ho ōn epi pantōn theos eulogētos eis tous aiōnas* (ὁ ὢν ἐπὶ πάντων θεὸς εὐλογητὸς εἰς τοὺς αἰῶνας) — the punctuation and syntax of this clause are debated. It can be read as: (1) a statement about Christ: "who is God over all, blessed forever" or (2) a doxology to the Father: "God who is over all be blessed forever." The grammar most naturally supports reading it as referring to Christ.
- v.6d: *ouch hoion de hoti ekpeptōken ho logos tou theou* — "It is not as though God's word has fallen/failed." This is the thesis of chapters 9–11: God hasn't broken his promises to Israel.
- v.7e: Quoted from Genesis 21:12. God's election has always worked through chosen lines, not biological descent alone.
- v.11f: *kat' eklogēn prothesis* (κατ᾽ ἐκλογὴν πρόθεσις) — "purpose according to election/choice." God's sovereign choice precedes and is independent of human action. This is the hardest part of Paul's argument for many readers.
- v.13g: Quoted from Malachi 1:2–3. "Hated" (*emisēsa*) in Hebrew idiom means "loved less" or "not chosen" rather than active hatred. The statement applies to the nations (Edom and Israel), not just to the individuals.
- v.15h: Quoted from Exodus 33:19. God's mercy is free — not arbitrary, but sovereign and not owed to anyone.
- v.20i: Paul echoes Isaiah 29:16 and 45:9. The potter/clay metaphor doesn't mean humans are mere objects — it means they're in no position to demand an explanation from their Creator.
- v.22j: *skeuē orgēs* (σκεύη ὀργῆς) — "vessels of wrath." The grammar is ambiguous: are they "made" for destruction by God, or have they "fitted themselves" for destruction? The middle voice *katērtismena* can support either reading. Paul's emphasis, however, is on God's patience, not on the mechanism of hardening.
- v.27k: *to hypoleimma* (τὸ ὑπόλειμμα) — "the remnant." A key prophetic concept: God always preserves a faithful core within Israel, even when the majority turns away.
- v.32l: *lithos proskommatos* (λίθος προσκόμματος) — Paul combines Isaiah 8:14 and 28:16 into a single quotation. Christ is simultaneously the foundation stone for faith and the stumbling block for unbelief.
