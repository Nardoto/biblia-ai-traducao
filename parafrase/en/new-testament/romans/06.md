# Romans 6

**1** So what do we say then? Should we keep on sinning so that grace keeps increasing? **2** Absolutely not! We died to sin — how can we still live in it? **3** Don't you know that all of us who were baptized into the Messiah Jesus were baptized into his death?ᵃ **4** We were buried with him through baptism into death, so that just as the Messiah was raised from the dead by the Father's glory, we too might walk in an entirely new kind of life.ᵇ

**5** Because if we've been united with him in a death like his, we'll certainly be united with him in a resurrection like his too. **6** We know this: our old selfᶜ was crucified with him, so that the body ruled by sin would be rendered powerless, and we'd no longer be enslaved to sin. **7** Because anyone who has died has been set freeᵈ from sin.

**8** Now if we died with the Messiah, we believe that we'll also live with him. **9** We know that since the Messiah has been raised from the dead, he can't die again — death no longer has any dominion over him. **10** The death he died, he died to sin once for all.ᵉ But the life he lives, he lives to God. **11** In the same way, count yourselves dead to sin but alive to God in the Messiah Jesus.

**12** So don't let sin reign in your mortal body, making you obey its cravings. **13** Don't offer the parts of your body to sin as weapons of unrighteousnessᶠ — instead, offer yourselves to God as people who've been brought from death to life, and offer the parts of your body to God as weapons of righteousness. **14** Sin won't be your master, because you're not under Law but under grace.

**15** So what then? Should we sin because we're not under Law but under grace? Absolutely not! **16** Don't you realize that when you offer yourselves to someone as obedient slaves, you're slaves of the one you obey — whether that's sin, which leads to death, or obedience, which leads to righteousness? **17** But thank God — you used to be slaves of sin, but you wholeheartedly obeyed the pattern of teachingᵍ you were handed over to. **18** You were set free from sin and became slaves of righteousness.

**19** I'm using everyday language because of your human limitations.ʰ Just as you once offered the parts of your body as slaves to impurity and to ever-increasing lawlessness, so now offer them as slaves to righteousness that leads to holiness.

**20** When you were slaves of sin, you were free from any obligation to righteousness. **21** And what fruit did you get from the things you're now ashamed of? Those things lead to death. **22** But now — set free from sin and enslaved to God — the fruit you get leads to holiness, and the outcome is eternal life. **23** Because the wages sin pays is death, but God's free giftⁱ is eternal life in the Messiah Jesus our Lord.

## Notes

- v.3a: *ebaptisthēmen eis ton thanaton autou* (ἐβαπτίσθημεν εἰς τὸν θάνατον αὐτοῦ) — baptism into Christ's death. Paul isn't merely describing a ritual but a real spiritual event: participation in Christ's death. The believer's union with Christ is so complete that what happened to him happened to them.
- v.4b: *kainotēti zōēs* (καινότητι ζωῆς) — "newness of life." *Kainotēs* means qualitative newness, not just chronological newness (*neos*). This is a fundamentally different kind of existence.
- v.6c: *palaios hēmōn anthrōpos* (παλαιὸς ἡμῶν ἄνθρωπος) — "our old person/self." Not just "former habits" but the entire former identity — the person we were in solidarity with Adam.
- v.7d: *dedikaiōtai apo tēs hamartias* (δεδικαίωται ἀπὸ τῆς ἁμαρτίας) — literally "has been justified from sin." Paul may be drawing on a rabbinic principle that death pays all debts. The dead person is free from sin's claims.
- v.10e: *ephapax* (ἐφάπαξ) — "once for all," a single unrepeatable event. Christ's death was definitive and complete.
- v.13f: *hopla* (ὅπλα) — can mean "tools" or "weapons." Given the military metaphor running through the passage (slavery, obedience, service), "weapons" captures the sense that Paul envisions spiritual warfare.
- v.17g: *typon didachēs* (τύπον διδαχῆς) — "pattern of teaching." This suggests an established body of Christian instruction that new converts were formed by — an early creed or catechism.
- v.19h: *anthrōpinon legō* (ἀνθρώπινον λέγω) — Paul apologizes for the slavery analogy. The comparison is imperfect: slavery to righteousness is actually freedom, not degradation. He uses the metaphor because it communicates the totality of commitment.
- v.23i: *charisma tou theou* (χάρισμα τοῦ θεοῦ) — Paul deliberately contrasts *opsōnia* (wages, a soldier's pay — what you earn) with *charisma* (a grace-gift — what you're given freely). Death is earned; life is given.
