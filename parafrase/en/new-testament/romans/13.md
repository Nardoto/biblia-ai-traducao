# Romans 13

**1** Every person should submit to the governing authorities. Because there's no authority except what God has established, and the authorities that exist have been put in place by God.ᵃ **2** So whoever resists the authority is resisting what God has set up, and those who resist will bring judgment on themselves. **3** Rulers aren't a source of fear for those doing good, but for those doing evil. You want to live without fearing the authority? Do what's good, and you'll receive its approval. **4** Because the authority is God's servantᵇ for your benefit. But if you do what's evil, be afraid — because it doesn't carry the sword for nothing. It's God's servant, an agent of wrath to bring punishment on the wrongdoer.

**5** That's why it's necessary to submit — not just because of wrath but also because of conscience. **6** This is also why you pay taxes, because the authorities are God's public servants,ᶜ devoted to this very purpose. **7** Give everyone what you owe them: taxes to whom taxes are owed, revenue to whom revenue is owed, respect to whom respect is owed, honor to whom honor is owed.

**8** Don't owe anyone anything — except the ongoing debt of loving one another.ᵈ Because the person who loves their neighbor has fulfilled the Law. **9** The commandments — "Don't commit adultery," "Don't murder," "Don't steal," "Don't covet" — and whatever other commandment there is — are all summed up in this one word: "Love your neighbor as yourself." **10** Love doesn't do harm to a neighbor. So love is the fulfillment of the Law.ᵉ

**11** And do all this understanding the present time: the hour has come for you to wake upᶠ from sleep, because our salvation is nearer now than when we first believed. **12** The night is almost over; the day is nearly here. So let's strip off the works of darknessᵍ and put on the armor of light. **13** Let's live decently, as in the daytime — not in wild partying and drunkenness, not in sexual immorality and shameless behavior, not in quarreling and jealousy. **14** Instead, clothe yourselves with the Lord Jesus the Messiah, and don't make any plans to gratify the cravings of the flesh.

## Notes

- v.1a: *exousiais hyperechousais* (ἐξουσίαις ὑπερεχούσαις) — "governing authorities." Paul writes this to Christians living under Nero's Rome. This passage has been among the most debated in Christian history. Paul is establishing a general principle about God's ordering of society, not providing a blanket endorsement of every governmental action. The broader biblical witness includes examples of justified resistance to unjust authority (the Hebrew midwives, Daniel, the apostles in Acts 5:29).
- v.4b: *diakonos* (διάκονος) — "servant/minister." The same word used for church servants. The governing authority is, whether it knows it or not, functioning within God's ordering of the world.
- v.6c: *leitourgoi* (λειτουργοί) — "public servants," from which we get "liturgy." This was a technical term in the Greek world for those who performed public service. Paul elevates tax collectors to the status of God's liturgical ministers.
- v.8d: *to allēlous agapan* (τὸ ἀλλήλους ἀγαπᾶν) — "to love one another." This is a debt that can never be paid off — it's always outstanding. Every other obligation can be settled; love is permanent.
- v.10e: *plērōma nomou hē agapē* (πλήρωμα νόμου ἡ ἀγάπη) — "love is the fulfillment of the Law." *Plērōma* means "fullness, completion." Love doesn't replace the Law — it's what the Law was always getting at.
- v.11f: *hōra ēdē hymas ex hypnou egerthēnai* (ὥρα ἤδη ὑμᾶς ἐξ ὕπνου ἐγερθῆναι) — the urgency of the eschatological moment. *Egerthēnai* ("to be raised/awakened") is the same verb used for resurrection — the wake-up call has resurrection overtones.
- v.12g: *ta erga tou skotous... ta hopla tou phōtos* (τὰ ἔργα τοῦ σκότους... τὰ ὅπλα τοῦ φωτός) — the contrast is between "works" (products of darkness) and "weapons/armor" (equipment of light). Christians don't just produce different things — they're equipped for a different kind of conflict.
