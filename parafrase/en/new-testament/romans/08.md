# Romans 8

**1** So now — no condemnation for those who are in the Messiah Jesus!ᵃ **2** Because the law of the Spirit of life in the Messiah Jesus has set you free from the law of sin and death. **3** What the Law couldn't do — weakened as it was by the fleshᵇ — God did. He sent his own Son in the likeness of sinful fleshᶜ, and as a sin offering, he condemned sin in the flesh, **4** so that the righteous requirement of the Law might be fully met in us — us who don't walk according to the flesh but according to the Spirit.

**5** Those who live according to the flesh set their minds on the things of the flesh, but those who live according to the Spirit set their minds on the things of the Spirit.ᵈ **6** The mindset of the flesh is death, but the mindset of the Spirit is life and peace. **7** Because the mindset of the flesh is hostile toward God — it doesn't submit to God's law and it can't. **8** Those who are in the flesh can't please God.

**9** But you aren't in the flesh — you're in the Spirit, since the Spirit of God lives in you. And if anyone doesn't have the Spirit of the Messiah, they don't belong to him. **10** But if the Messiah is in you, then even though the body is dead because of sin, the Spirit means life because of righteousness. **11** And if the Spirit of the one who raised Jesus from the dead lives in you, then the one who raised the Messiah from the dead will also give life to your mortal bodies through his Spirit who lives in you.

**12** So then, brothers and sisters, we're under obligation — but not to the flesh, to live according to the flesh. **13** Because if you live according to the flesh, you're going to die. But if by the Spirit you put to death the practices of the body, you'll live. **14** Because all who are led by the Spirit of God — these are God's sons and daughters. **15** You didn't receive a spirit of slaveryᵉ that drags you back into fear. Instead, you received the Spirit of adoption,ᶠ by whom we cry out, "Abba! Father!" **16** The Spirit himself testifies together with our spirit that we are God's children. **17** And if children, then heirs — heirs of God and co-heirs with the Messiah, since we suffer with him so that we may also be glorified with him.

**18** I'm convinced that the sufferings of this present time aren't worth comparing with the glory that's going to be revealed to us. **19** The creation itself is standing on tiptoe,ᵍ eagerly waiting for the sons and daughters of God to be revealed. **20** Because creation was subjected to futilityʰ — not by its own choice but by the will of the one who subjected it — in hope **21** that creation itself will be set free from its slavery to decay and brought into the glorious freedom of God's children.

**22** We know that the whole creation has been groaning together in the pains of childbirth right up to now. **23** And not just creation — we ourselves, who have the firstfruitsⁱ of the Spirit, groan inwardly as we eagerly wait for our adoption, the redemption of our bodies. **24** Because we were saved in this hope. But hope that you can see isn't really hope — who hopes for what they can already see? **25** But if we hope for what we don't yet see, we wait for it with patience.

**26** In the same way, the Spirit helps us in our weakness. We don't know how to pray as we should, but the Spirit himself intercedes for us with groansʲ too deep for words. **27** And the one who searches hearts knows the mind of the Spirit, because the Spirit intercedes for God's people according to God's will.

**28** And we know that for those who love God, all things work togetherᵏ for good — for those who are called according to his purpose. **29** Because those God knew in advance, he also marked out in advanceˡ to be shaped into the image of his Son, so that he'd be the firstborn among many brothers and sisters. **30** And those he marked out in advance, he also called; those he called, he also declared righteous; and those he declared righteous, he also glorified.ᵐ

**31** So what do we say in response to all this? If God is for us, who can be against us? **32** He who didn't spare his own Son but handed him over for all of us — how will he not also, along with him, freely give us everything? **33** Who will bring any charge against God's chosen ones? God is the one who declares righteous. **34** Who is the one who condemns? The Messiah Jesus is the one who died — and more than that, was raised — who is at the right hand of God, who is actually interceding for us.

**35** Who will separate us from the Messiah's love? Will trouble? Or hardship? Or persecution? Or famine? Or nakedness? Or danger? Or sword? **36** As it's written: "For your sake we face death all day long; we're considered sheep to be slaughtered." **37** No — in all these things we are more than conquerors through the one who loved us. **38** Because I'm absolutely convinced that neither death nor life, neither angels nor rulers, neither things present nor things to come, nor any powers, **39** neither height nor depth, nor anything else in all creation will be able to separate us from the love of God that is in the Messiah Jesus our Lord.

## Notes

- v.1a: *Ouden ara nyn katakrima* (Οὐδὲν ἄρα νῦν κατάκριμα) — "Therefore now no condemnation." The word *ara* ("therefore") connects this to everything Paul has argued since chapter 1. This is the verdict the whole letter has been building toward.
- v.3b: *to adynaton tou nomou en hō ēsthenei dia tēs sarkos* — the Law wasn't defective in itself; it was powerless because it had to work through fallen human nature.
- v.3c: *en homoiōmati sarkos hamartias* (ἐν ὁμοιώματι σαρκὸς ἁμαρτίας) — "in the likeness of sinful flesh." Paul's language is precise: Christ took on real human nature (*sarx*) but not the sin that corrupts it. *Homoiōma* indicates genuine similarity without complete identity.
- v.5d: *phronousin* (φρονοῦσιν) — more than just "think about." This is orientation, mindset, the fundamental direction of one's desires and attention.
- v.15e: *pneuma douleias* (πνεῦμα δουλείας) — "spirit of slavery." This evokes Israel's slavery in Egypt. The Exodus pattern runs throughout: God liberates his people from bondage.
- v.15f: *pneuma huiothesias* (πνεῦμα υἱοθεσίας) — "Spirit of adoption." *Huiothesia* was a Roman legal term for the formal adoption of a son, conferring full inheritance rights. *Abba* is the Aramaic word for "father" — intimate, familial, the word Jesus himself used in prayer (Mark 14:36).
- v.19g: *apokaradokia* (ἀποκαραδοκία) — a vivid compound word meaning "eager expectation," literally craning one's neck to see something approaching. Creation is personified as straining forward on its toes.
- v.20h: *mataiotēti* (ματαιότητι) — "futility/frustration." This echoes the "vanity" (*hebel*) of Ecclesiastes and the curse of Genesis 3. Creation is caught in a cycle of decay that isn't its fault.
- v.23i: *aparchēn tou pneumatos* (ἀπαρχὴν τοῦ πνεύματος) — "firstfruits of the Spirit." The Spirit is the first installment of the coming harvest — a down payment guaranteeing the full inheritance.
- v.26j: *stenagmois alalētois* (στεναγμοῖς ἀλαλήτοις) — "groans that can't be put into words." The Spirit's intercession goes deeper than human language can reach.
- v.28k: *synergei eis agathon* (συνεργεῖ εἰς ἀγαθόν) — some manuscripts read "God works all things together for good." Either way, Paul isn't saying everything is good — he's saying God is working within all circumstances toward an ultimate good purpose.
- v.29l: *proōrisen* (προώρισεν) — "predetermined/marked out in advance." Paul's focus here isn't on the mechanics of predestination but on the security of God's people: the process God started, he'll finish.
- v.30m: *edoxasen* (ἐδόξασεν) — "glorified." The aorist tense is striking: Paul speaks of glorification as already accomplished, though it's still future from our perspective. In God's purpose, it's as good as done.
