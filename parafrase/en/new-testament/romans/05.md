# Romans 5

**1** So since we've been declared righteous by faith, we have peaceᵃ with God through our Lord Jesus the Messiah. **2** Through him we've also gained accessᵇ by faith into this grace where we now stand. And we boast in the hope of sharing God's glory.

**3** Not only that — we even boast in our suffering, because we know that suffering produces endurance,ᶜ **4** endurance produces proven character,ᵈ and proven character produces hope. **5** And this hope doesn't disappoint us, because God's loveᵉ has been poured out in our hearts through the Holy Spirit who was given to us.

**6** You see, at just the right momentᶠ — when we were still powerless — Christ died for the ungodly. **7** It's rare enough for someone to die for a righteous person — though maybe for a truly good person someone might actually dare to die. **8** But God demonstrates his own love for us in this: while we were still sinners, Christ died for us.

**9** So if we've now been declared righteous by his blood, how much more will we be saved from the wrathᵍ through him! **10** Because if, while we were enemies, we were reconciledʰ to God through the death of his Son, how much more — now that we're reconciled — will we be saved through his life! **11** And it doesn't stop there — we actually boast in God through our Lord Jesus the Messiah, through whom we've now received this reconciliation.

**12** So here's how it works: sin entered the world through one man, and death came through sin, and in this way death spread to every human being, because everyone sinned.ⁱ **13** Sin was already in the world before the Law came — but sin isn't charged to anyone's account where there's no law. **14** Still, death reigned from Adam to Moses, even over those who didn't sin in the same way Adam did — by disobeying a direct command. And Adam is a patternʲ of the one who was to come.

**15** But the free gift isn't like the trespass. If the many died because of one man's trespass, how much more has God's grace — the gift given through the grace of one man, Jesus the Messiah — overflowed to the many! **16** And the gift isn't like what came through the one who sinned: the judgment that followed one trespass brought condemnation, but the free gift that followed many trespasses brought a verdict of righteousness.ᵏ **17** If death reigned through the one man because of his one trespass, how much more will those who receive the overflow of grace and the gift of righteousness reign in life through the one man — Jesus the Messiah!

**18** So then, just as one trespass brought condemnation for everyone, so one act of righteousnessˡ brought justification and life for everyone. **19** Just as through one man's disobedience the many were made sinners, so through one man's obedience the many will be made righteous.

**20** The Law slipped in alongside so that the trespass would increase.ᵐ But where sin increased, grace overflowed even more — **21** so that, just as sin reigned through death, grace would also reign through righteousness, leading to eternal life through Jesus the Messiah, our Lord.

## Notes

- v.1a: *eirēnēn* (εἰρήνην) — not just the absence of hostility but the Hebrew concept of *shalom*: wholeness, well-being, a fully restored relationship. Some manuscripts read the subjunctive *echōmen* ("let us have peace") rather than the indicative *echomen* ("we have peace") — a single letter's difference. Most scholars favor the indicative as a declaration.
- v.2b: *prosagōgēn* (προσαγωγήν) — a technical term for being introduced into the presence of a king. The image is of being ushered into God's throne room and being told you can stay.
- v.3c: *hypomonēn* (ὑπομονήν) — not passive resignation but active endurance, the ability to stand firm under pressure without breaking.
- v.4d: *dokimēn* (δοκιμήν) — character that has been tested and approved, like metal refined by fire and proven genuine. Not merely "experience" but the verified result of having endured the trial.
- v.5e: *agapē tou theou* (ἀγάπη τοῦ θεοῦ) — "God's love." Can mean "the love God has for us" or "the love God enables us to have." Context (vv. 6–8) makes the primary meaning God's love for us.
- v.6f: *kata kairon* (κατὰ καιρόν) — not *chronos* (clock time) but *kairos*: the decisive moment, the opportune time in God's plan.
- v.9g: *orgēs* (ὀργῆς) — God's wrath, his settled opposition to everything destructive, already introduced in 1:18.
- v.10h: *katēllagēmen* (κατηλλάγημεν) — from *katallassō*: to restore a broken relationship. The initiative is entirely God's — he reconciled his enemies to himself.
- v.12i: *eph' hō pantes hēmarton* (ἐφ᾽ ᾧ πάντες ἥμαρτον) — one of the most debated phrases in the NT. It can mean "because all sinned" (causal), "on the basis that all sinned," or "in whom [Adam] all sinned." The causal reading is the most natural grammatically.
- v.14j: *typos* (τύπος) — "type," "pattern." Adam foreshadows Christ by contrast: where Adam brought death, Christ brings life.
- v.16k: *dikaiōma* (δικαίωμα) — here meaning "verdict of righteousness" or "justification." Paul contrasts *katakrima* (condemnation) with *dikaiōma* (acquittal).
- v.18l: *dikaiōma* (δικαίωμα) — here in a different sense from v.16: "righteous act," referring to Christ's obedience unto death. The same Greek word shifts in nuance within three verses.
- v.20m: *pareisēlthen* (παρεισῆλθεν) — the prefix *para-* ("alongside") + *eis-* ("into") gives the sense of "slipped in by a side door." The Law entered as a supporting player, not the main character — and its paradoxical role was to magnify the problem so the solution could be seen more clearly.
