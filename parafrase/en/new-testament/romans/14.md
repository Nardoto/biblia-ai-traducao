# Romans 14

**1** Welcome the person who is weakᵃ in faith — but not for the purpose of arguing about debatable matters. **2** One person believes they can eat anything, while the weak person eats only vegetables. **3** The one who eats must not look down on the one who doesn't, and the one who doesn't eat must not judge the one who does — because God has welcomed that person. **4** Who are you to judge someone else's household servant?ᵇ It's before their own master that they stand or fall. And they will stand — because the Lord has the power to make them stand.

**5** One person considers one day more sacred than another; another person considers every day alike. Each one should be fully convincedᶜ in their own mind. **6** The one who observes a special day does it for the Lord. The one who eats, eats for the Lord, since they give thanks to God. And the one who doesn't eat, it's for the Lord that they don't eat, and they give thanks to God too.

**7** None of us lives for ourselves alone, and none of us dies for ourselves alone. **8** If we live, we live for the Lord; if we die, we die for the Lord. So whether we live or die, we belong to the Lord. **9** This is why the Messiah died and came back to life — so that he might be Lord of both the dead and the living.

**10** So why do you judge your brother or sister? Or why do you look down on your brother or sister? We'll all stand before God's judgment seat.ᵈ **11** As it's written: "As surely as I live, says the Lord, every knee will bow before me, and every tongue will confess to God." **12** So then, each of us will give an account of ourselves to God.

**13** Therefore, let's stop judging one another. Instead, make up your mind not to put a stumbling block or an obstacleᵉ in your brother or sister's way. **14** I know and am convinced in the Lord Jesus that nothing is uncleanᶠ in itself. But if someone considers something unclean, then for that person it is unclean. **15** If your brother or sister is being hurt by what you eat, you're no longer walking in love. Don't destroy by your food someone for whom the Messiah died! **16** Don't let what you consider good be spoken of as evil. **17** Because the kingdom of God isn't about eating and drinking — it's about righteousness, peace, and joy in the Holy Spirit. **18** Whoever serves the Messiah in this way is pleasing to God and approved by people.

**19** So then, let's pursue the things that lead to peace and to building each other up.ᵍ **20** Don't tear down God's work for the sake of food. Everything is clean — but it's wrong for a person to eat something that causes someone else to stumble. **21** It's better not to eat meat or drink wine or do anything that causes your brother or sister to stumble.

**22** Whatever faith you have on these matters, keep it between yourself and God. Blessed is the person who doesn't condemn themselves by what they approve. **23** But the person who has doubts is condemned if they eat, because their eating doesn't come from faith. And everything that doesn't come from faith is sin.ʰ

## Notes

- v.1a: *asthenounta tē pistei* (ἀσθενοῦντα τῇ πίστει) — "weak in faith." The "weak" person isn't spiritually inferior — they're someone whose conscience hasn't yet grasped the full freedom that comes in the Messiah. Paul's sympathies are with the "strong" (15:1), but his instructions protect the weak.
- v.4b: *oiketēn* (οἰκέτην) — "household slave." The metaphor is pointed: you wouldn't presume to discipline someone else's household staff. That's the master's prerogative.
- v.5c: *plērophoreisthō* (πληροφορείσθω) — "be fully convinced/assured." Paul doesn't demand uniformity on secondary matters — he demands that each person act from genuine conviction.
- v.10d: *bēmati tou theou* (βήματι τοῦ θεοῦ) — "judgment seat of God." The *bēma* was the raised platform where a magistrate rendered verdicts. Some manuscripts read "judgment seat of Christ" (cf. 2 Corinthians 5:10).
- v.13e: *proskomma... skandalon* (πρόσκομμα... σκάνδαλον) — two synonyms piled up: a stumbling block and a trap/snare. Paul is emphatic: don't become the reason someone else falls.
- v.14f: *koinon* (κοινόν) — "common/unclean" in the Jewish ritual sense. Paul affirms the Messiah's teaching (Mark 7:19) that no food is inherently defiling. But he immediately qualifies this with the principle of conscience.
- v.19g: *oikodomēs* (οἰκοδομῆς) — "building up/edification." The metaphor is architectural: you're constructing a community, not tearing it apart over dietary choices.
- v.23h: *pan de ho ouk ek pisteōs hamartia estin* (πᾶν δὲ ὃ οὐκ ἐκ πίστεως ἁμαρτία ἐστίν) — "everything that is not from faith is sin." This is not a universal philosophical statement but applies specifically to the matter at hand: eating with a guilty conscience violates one's own integrity before God.
