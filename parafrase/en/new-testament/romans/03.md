# Romans 3

**1** So what's the advantage of being a Jew? What's the point of circumcision? **2** A lot, actually — in every way! First and foremost, the Jews were entrusted with the very words of God.ᵃ **3** So what if some of them were unfaithful? Does their unfaithfulness cancel out God's faithfulness? **4** Absolutely not! Let God be true even if every human being is a liar — just as it's written: "So that you may be proven right when you speak and prevail when you are judged."ᵇ

**5** But if our unrighteousness highlights God's righteousness, what do we say? Is God unjust for inflicting wrath? (I'm speaking in purely human terms.) **6** Absolutely not! Otherwise, how could God judge the world? **7** "But if God's truth is amplified by my falsehood, bringing him greater glory, why am I still being condemned as a sinner?" **8** And why not say — as some people slander us by claiming we say — "Let's do evil so that good may come"? Their condemnation is well deserved.

**9** So what then? Are we Jews any better off? Not at all! We've already made the charge that Jews and Greeks alike are all under the power of sin.ᶜ **10** As it's written:

"There's no one righteous — not even one.
**11** There's no one who understands,
no one who seeks God.
**12** They've all turned away,
they've all become worthless together.
There's no one who does good —
not even one."
**13** "Their throats are open graves,
their tongues practice deceit."
"The venom of vipers is under their lips."
**14** "Their mouths are full of cursing and bitterness."
**15** "Their feet are quick to shed blood,
**16** ruin and misery mark their path,
**17** and the way of peace they've never known."
**18** "There is no fear of God before their eyes."

**19** Now we know that everything the Law says, it says to those who are under the Law, so that every mouth may be silenced and the whole world held accountable to God. **20** Because no human being will be declared righteous before him by doing what the Law demands — what the Law actually does is make us fully aware of sin.ᵈ

**21** But now — apart from the Law entirely — God's righteousness has been revealed, and the Law and the Prophets themselves testify to it. **22** This righteousness from God comes through faithᵉ in Jesus the Messiah to all who believe. There's no distinction — **23** because everyone has sinned and falls short of God's glory. **24** And they're declared righteous freely, by his grace, through the redemptionᶠ that's in the Messiah Jesus. **25** God put him forward as a hilastērionᵍ — an atoning sacrifice — through faith, by his blood. He did this to demonstrate his righteousness, because in his patience he had passed over sins committed earlier — **26** to demonstrate his righteousness at the present time, so that he would be both righteous himself and the one who declares righteous the person who has faith in Jesus.

**27** So where's the boasting? It's been shut out. By what kind of law? A law of works? No — by the law of faith. **28** Because we hold that a person is declared righteous by faith, apart from doing what the Law demands.ʰ **29** Or is God the God of Jews only? Isn't he the God of Gentiles too? Yes — of Gentiles too! **30** Since God is one — and he'll declare the circumcised righteous on the basis of faith and the uncircumcised righteous through that same faith. **31** So are we abolishing the Law through faith? Absolutely not! We're actually upholding it.

## Notes

- v.2a: *ta logia tou theou* (τὰ λόγια τοῦ θεοῦ) — "the oracles of God," referring to the entire body of divine revelation entrusted to Israel, including the Scriptures, promises, and covenants.
- v.4b: Quoted from Psalm 51:4 (LXX 50:6) — David's confession. Paul uses it to establish that God is always vindicated when his faithfulness is questioned.
- v.9c: *hyph' hamartian* (ὑφ᾽ ἁμαρτίαν) — "under sin." Sin here is personified as a ruling power, not just individual acts. This sets up Paul's fuller treatment in chapters 5–7.
- v.20d: *epignōsis hamartias* (ἐπίγνωσις ἁμαρτίας) — "full knowledge of sin." The Law's role is diagnostic, not therapeutic — it identifies the disease but doesn't cure it.
- v.22e: *dia pisteōs Iēsou Christou* (διὰ πίστεως Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ) — this can mean "through faith in Jesus Christ" (objective genitive) or "through the faithfulness of Jesus Christ" (subjective genitive). Both readings have strong scholarly support. The paraphrase keeps the traditional reading while acknowledging the ambiguity.
- v.24f: *apolytrōseōs* (ἀπολυτρώσεως) — "redemption," from the marketplace of slave liberation. The price is paid and the captive goes free.
- v.25g: *hilastērion* (ἱλαστήριον) — one of the most debated words in Romans. It can mean: (1) "mercy seat" — the gold lid of the Ark of the Covenant where God's presence met Israel on the Day of Atonement (its primary LXX usage); (2) "propitiation" — that which turns away God's wrath; (3) "expiation" — that which cleanses sin. Paul likely intends to evoke the Day of Atonement imagery: Jesus is the new mercy seat where God and humanity meet.
- v.28h: *chōris ergōn nomou* (χωρὶς ἔργων νόμου) — "apart from works of the Law." This is the core of Paul's argument and became the flashpoint of the Reformation. "Works of the Law" refers to Torah observance as a basis for being declared righteous, not to good works in general.
