# Romans 7

**1** Don't you know, brothers and sisters — I'm speaking to people who know the law — that the law has authority over a person only as long as they're alive? **2** For example, a married woman is legally bound to her husband while he's alive. But if the husband dies, she's released from the law regarding her husband.ᵃ **3** So if she lives with another man while her husband is alive, she'll be called an adulteress. But if her husband dies, she's free from that law, and she's not an adulteress even if she marries another man.

**4** In the same way, my brothers and sisters, you too have diedᵇ to the Law through the body of the Messiah, so that you could belong to another — to the one who was raised from the dead — in order that we might bear fruit for God. **5** When we were living according to the fleshᶜ, the sinful passions stirred up by the Law were at work in the parts of our body, bearing fruit for death. **6** But now we've been released from the Law, having died to what once held us captive, so that we serve in the new way of the Spiritᵈ and not in the old way of the written code.

**7** So what are we saying — that the Law is sin? Absolutely not! But I wouldn't have known sin except through the Law. I wouldn't have known what covetingᵉ was if the Law hadn't said, "You shall not covet." **8** But sin, seizing its opportunity through the commandment, produced in me every kind of coveting. Without the Law, sin is dead. **9** I was once alive apart from the Law, but when the commandment came, sin sprang to life **10** and I died. The very commandment that was supposed to bring life turned out to bring death for me.ᶠ **11** Because sin, seizing its opportunity through the commandment, deceived me and through it killed me.

**12** So the Law itself is holy, and the commandment is holy, righteous, and good. **13** Did what is good, then, become death for me? Absolutely not! It was sin — so that sin might be exposed as sin by producing death in me through what is good — so that through the commandment, sin might become utterly sinful.ᵍ

**14** We know that the Law is spiritual, but I am made of fleshʰ, sold as a slave under sin's power. **15** I don't understand what I do. What I want to do, I don't do. What I hate, I end up doing. **16** And if I do what I don't want to do, I'm agreeing that the Law is good. **17** But as it stands, it's no longer I who do it but sin living in me. **18** I know that nothing good lives in me — that is, in my fleshⁱ. The desire to do what's right is there, but I can't carry it out. **19** I don't do the good I want to do; instead, I do the evil I don't want to do. **20** And if I do what I don't want to do, it's no longer I who do it but sin living in me.

**21** So I find this principle at work: when I want to do good, evil is right there with me. **22** In my inner being I delight in God's law. **23** But I see a different lawʲ at work in the parts of my body, waging war against the law of my mind and taking me prisoner to the law of sin that operates in the parts of my body. **24** What a wretched person I am! Who will rescue me from this body of death? **25** Thanks be to God — through Jesus the Messiah our Lord!

So then, with my mind I myself serve God's law, but with my flesh I serve the law of sin.

---

## Notes

- v.2a: Paul's analogy isn't meant to be allegorically precise in every detail. The point is singular: death breaks legal obligations. Believers have died (with Christ) and are therefore free from the Law's binding authority.
- v.4b: *ethanatōthēte* (ἐθανατώθητε) — "you were put to death." The passive voice is significant: believers didn't free themselves from the Law; they were freed through Christ's death.
- v.5c: *en tē sarki* (ἐν τῇ σαρκί) — "in the flesh." *Sarx* for Paul doesn't mean "body" but the whole human existence oriented away from God — human nature in its weakness and rebellion.
- v.6d: *en kainotēti pneumatos* (ἐν καινότητι πνεύματος) — "in newness of Spirit." The contrast between Spirit and written code (*gramma*) isn't between spiritual and literal readings of Scripture, but between two different eras and modes of relating to God.
- v.7e: *epithymian* (ἐπιθυμίαν) — "coveting/desire." Paul chooses the tenth commandment because it's the most internal — it addresses the hidden movements of the heart, not just outward actions.
- v.10f: The commandment was *eis zōēn* ("unto life," Leviticus 18:5) — it promised life to those who kept it. But because of sin's power, it produced the opposite result.
- v.13g: *kath' hyperbolēn hamartōlos* (καθ᾽ ὑπερβολὴν ἁμαρτωλός) — "sinful beyond measure." The Law unmasks sin's true hideousness. This is actually part of God's plan — you can't treat a disease you can't diagnose.
- v.14h: *sarkinos* (σάρκινος) — "made of flesh," fleshly in constitution. This is different from *sarkikos* ("fleshly in behavior"). Paul is describing the human condition, not moral failure.
- v.18i: The qualifier "that is, in my flesh" is crucial. Paul isn't saying the whole person is irredeemable, but that the flesh — the unredeemed aspect of human existence — contains no resources for doing good.
- v.23j: Paul uses *nomos* ("law") in multiple senses in rapid succession: God's law, the principle of sin, the law of the mind. This wordplay is intentional — the very concept of "law" is being stretched to show the complexity of the human predicament.
