# Romans 11

**1** So I ask: did God reject his people? Absolutely not! I myself am an Israelite, a descendant of Abraham, from the tribe of Benjamin. **2** God didn't reject his people, whom he knew in advance.ᵃ Don't you know what the Scripture says in the passage about Elijah — how he pleads with God against Israel? **3** "Lord, they've killed your prophets, they've torn down your altars, and I'm the only one left, and they're trying to kill me too." **4** But what's God's answer to him? "I've kept for myself seven thousand men who haven't bowed the knee to Baal."ᵇ

**5** In the same way, at the present time there's a remnantᶜ chosen by grace. **6** And if it's by grace, then it's no longer based on works — otherwise grace would no longer be grace.

**7** So what then? Israel didn't obtain what it was seeking. The elect obtained it, but the rest were hardened,ᵈ **8** as it's written: "God gave them a spirit of stupor — eyes that don't see and ears that don't hear — right up to this very day." **9** And David says: "Let their table become a snare and a trap, a stumbling block and a payback for them. **10** Let their eyes be darkened so they can't see, and bend their backs forever."

**11** So I ask: did they stumble so as to fall beyond recovery? Absolutely not! Rather, through their trespass, salvation has come to the Gentiles, to make Israel jealous.ᵉ **12** Now if their trespass means riches for the world, and their diminishing means riches for the Gentiles, how much more will their full inclusion mean!

**13** I'm speaking to you Gentiles now. Insofar as I'm an apostle to the Gentiles, I make much of my ministry — **14** in the hope that I might somehow make my own people jealous and save some of them. **15** Because if their rejection meant the reconciliation of the world, what will their acceptance mean except life from the dead?ᶠ

**16** If the first portion of dough offered is holy, then the whole batch is holy. And if the root is holy, so are the branches.ᵍ **17** Now if some of the branches have been broken off, and you — a wild olive shoot — have been grafted in among them and now share in the rich root of the olive tree, **18** don't boast over those branches. If you do boast, remember: you don't support the root — the root supports you.

**19** You'll say, "Branches were broken off so that I could be grafted in." **20** True enough — they were broken off because of unbelief, and you stand by faith. Don't be arrogant; be afraid.ʰ **21** Because if God didn't spare the natural branches, he won't spare you either. **22** Consider, then, both the kindnessⁱ and the severity of God: severity toward those who fell, but God's kindness toward you — provided you continue in his kindness. Otherwise, you too will be cut off. **23** And if they don't persist in unbelief, they'll be grafted back in, because God has the power to graft them in again. **24** After all, if you were cut from what is by nature a wild olive tree and grafted — against nature — into a cultivated olive tree, how much more readily will these natural branches be grafted back into their own olive tree!

**25** Brothers and sisters, I don't want you to be unaware of this mysteryʲ — so you won't become wise in your own estimation: a partial hardening has come upon Israel until the full number of the Gentiles has come in. **26** And in this way, all Israel will be saved,ᵏ as it's written: "The Deliverer will come from Zion; he will remove ungodliness from Jacob. **27** And this is my covenant with them, when I take away their sins."

**28** As far as the good news is concerned, they're enemies for your sake. But as far as election is concerned, they're loved because of the patriarchs. **29** Because God's gifts and his callingˡ are irrevocable.

**30** Just as you once were disobedient to God but have now received mercy through their disobedience, **31** so they too have now become disobedient, so that through the mercy shown to you they also may receive mercy. **32** Because God has locked up everyone in disobedienceᵐ so that he might show mercy to everyone.

**33** Oh, the depth of the riches and the wisdom and the knowledge of God! How unsearchable are his judgments and how untraceable his paths! **34** "Who has known the mind of the Lord? Or who has been his advisor?" **35** "Who has ever given something to God first, that God should repay them?" **36** Because from him and through him and for him are all things. To him be the glory forever! Amen.

## Notes

- v.2a: *proegnō* (προέγνω) — "foreknew." Not merely intellectual awareness but relational knowledge — God's prior, covenantal commitment to Israel.
- v.4b: Quoted from 1 Kings 19:18. God always preserves a faithful remnant, even when the prophet can't see it.
- v.5c: *leimma kat' eklogēn charitos* (λεῖμμα κατ᾽ ἐκλογὴν χάριτος) — "a remnant according to the election of grace." The remnant exists not because of their superior faithfulness but because of God's gracious choice.
- v.7d: *epōrōthēsan* (ἐπωρώθησαν) — "were hardened," literally "petrified" like calcium deposits. The divine passive suggests God as the agent, echoing the Pharaoh narrative of chapter 9.
- v.11e: *parazēlōsai* (παραζηλῶσαι) — "to provoke to jealousy." Paul envisions a divine strategy: Gentile inclusion isn't an afterthought but a means to stir Israel's longing for what they've been missing.
- v.15f: *zōē ek nekrōn* (ζωὴ ἐκ νεκρῶν) — "life from the dead." This could mean spiritual revival, bodily resurrection, or the cosmic renewal associated with the end times. Paul may deliberately leave the phrase evocative rather than precise.
- v.16g: *aparchē* (ἀπαρχή) — "firstfruits" of dough, referring to Numbers 15:20–21. The "root" likely refers to the patriarchs (Abraham, Isaac, Jacob), whose holiness consecrates the entire tree of Israel.
- v.20h: *mē hypsēla phronei alla phobou* (μὴ ὑψηλὰ φρόνει ἀλλὰ φοβοῦ) — "don't think lofty thoughts but fear." Paul warns Gentile Christians against the very arrogance toward Jews that tragically became common in later church history.
- v.22i: *chrēstotēta kai apotomian* (χρηστότητα καὶ ἀποτομίαν) — "kindness and severity." *Apotomia* literally means "cutting off" — a vivid term given the pruning imagery.
- v.25j: *mystērion* (μυστήριον) — in Paul's usage, not something mysterious but a previously hidden truth now revealed by God. This is the climactic revelation of chapters 9–11.
- v.26k: *pas Israēl sōthēsetai* (πᾶς Ἰσραὴλ σωθήσεται) — "all Israel will be saved." One of the most debated phrases in Paul. It could mean: (1) ethnic Israel as a whole will eventually turn to Christ, (2) the sum total of elect Jews across history, or (3) the full people of God (Jews and Gentiles). Most scholars read it as a future mass turning of ethnic Israel to their Messiah.
- v.29l: *ametamelēta* (ἀμεταμέλητα) — "not to be repented of," irrevocable. God doesn't take back what he gives or retract who he calls. This is the theological foundation for ongoing hope for Israel.
- v.32m: *synekleisen... tous pantas eis apeitheian* (συνέκλεισεν... τοὺς πάντας εἰς ἀπείθειαν) — "shut up all in disobedience." The purpose is not punishment but mercy — God confined everyone under the same condition so that salvation would be entirely by grace for everyone.
