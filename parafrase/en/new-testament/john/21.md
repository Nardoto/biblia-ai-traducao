# John 21

**1** After this, Jesus showed himself again to the followers at the Sea of Tiberias. Here's how it happened.

**2** Simon Peter, Thomas the Twin, Nathanael from Cana in Galilee, the sons of Zebedee, and two other followers were together. **3** Simon Peter said, "I'm going fishing." The others said, "We'll come with you." They went out and got into the boat, but that night they caught nothing.

**4** When dawn broke, Jesus was standing on the shore — but the followers didn't realize it was him. **5** Jesus called out, "Friends, haven't you caught any fish?"

"No," they answered.

**6** He said, "Throw your net on the right side of the boat and you'll find some." They threw it, and they couldn't haul it back in because of the massive number of fish.

**7** The follower Jesus was closest to said to Peter, "It's the Lord!" The moment Simon Peter heard it was the Lord, he threw on his outer garment — he'd stripped it off for work — and jumped into the water. **8** The other followers came in the boat, dragging the net full of fish. They weren't far from shore — about a hundred yards.

**9** When they landed, they saw a charcoal fireᵃ with fish on it, and bread. **10** Jesus said, "Bring some of the fish you just caught." **11** Simon Peter climbed back into the boat and dragged the net ashore. It was full of large fish — a hundred and fifty-three of them. And even with that many, the net didn't tear.

**12** Jesus said, "Come have breakfast." None of the followers dared to ask him, "Who are you?" They knew it was the Lord. **13** Jesus came over, took the bread, and gave it to them — and the fish too. **14** This was now the third time Jesus appeared to the followers after being raised from the dead.

**15** After breakfast, Jesus said to Simon Peter, "Simon, son of John, do you love me with everything you have — more than these?"ᵇ

Peter said, "Yes, Lord, you know I'm fond of you."ᶜ

Jesus said, "Feed my lambs."

**16** He asked a second time, "Simon, son of John, do you love me with everything you have?"

Peter said, "Yes, Lord, you know I'm fond of you."

Jesus said, "Shepherd my sheep."

**17** He asked a third time, "Simon, son of John, are you fond of me?"ᵈ

Peter was hurt because Jesus asked him a third time. He said, "Lord, you know everything. You know I'm fond of you."

Jesus said, "Feed my sheep.

**18** "I'm telling you the truth: when you were young, you dressed yourself and went wherever you wanted. But when you're old, you'll stretch out your hands, and someone else will dress you and take you where you don't want to go." **19** He said this to indicate the kind of death by which Peter would glorify God.ᵉ

Then he said to him, "Follow me."

**20** Peter turned around and saw the follower Jesus was closest to following behind them — the one who had leaned against Jesus' chest at the dinner and asked, "Lord, who's going to betray you?" **21** When Peter saw him, he asked Jesus, "Lord, what about him?"

**22** Jesus said, "If I want him to remain until I come back, what's that to you? You follow me." **23** Because of this, a rumor spread among the brothers and sisters that this follower wouldn't die. But Jesus hadn't said he wouldn't die — he'd said, "If I want him to remain until I come back, what's that to you?"

**24** This is the follower who testifies to these things and who wrote them down. And we know that his testimony is true.

**25** Jesus did many other things as well. If every one of them were written down, I suppose the whole world couldn't contain the books that would be written.

## Notes

- v.9a: ἀνθρακιάν (anthrakian) — "charcoal fire." The same word used in 18:18, where Peter warmed himself by a charcoal fire while denying Jesus. The detail is deliberate — Jesus restores Peter at the same kind of fire where Peter fell. John is the only Gospel writer who uses this specific word.
- v.15b: ἀγαπᾷς με πλέον τούτων — "Do you love (agapaō) me more than these?" The "these" is ambiguous — it could mean "more than these other followers love me" or "more than you love these things (the fishing life)." The paraphrase leaves the ambiguity open.
- v.15c: φιλῶ σε (philō se) — Peter responds with phileō (warm personal affection) rather than agapaō (the self-giving love Jesus asked about). Whether this distinction is theologically significant or simply stylistic variation has been debated for centuries. The paraphrase preserves the distinction ("love with everything you have" vs. "fond of you") so the reader can decide.
- v.17d: In the third question, Jesus switches to Peter's own word — φιλεῖς με (phileis me) — "are you even fond of me?" This is what wounds Peter: Jesus has come down to meet him at his own level, and even that lesser claim is being questioned. Three denials, three restorations.
- v.19e: The tradition that Peter was crucified upside down in Rome is attested by early church writers (Clement of Rome, Tertullian, Eusebius). "Stretch out your hands" was understood as a reference to crucifixion.
- v.25: A literary hyperbole (ὑπερβολή) expressing that no written account can fully capture who Jesus is and what he did. The Gospel is selective and purposeful, not exhaustive.
