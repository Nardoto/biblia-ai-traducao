# John 2

**1** Two days later, there was a wedding in Cana — a little town in Galilee — and Jesus' mother was there. **2** Jesus and his followers had been invited too. **3** At some point during the celebration, the wine ran out. Jesus' mother came over to him and said, "They've got no more wine."

**4** Jesus said to her, "Mom, why are you bringing this to me? It's not my time yet."

**5** But his mother just turned to the servants and said, "Whatever he tells you to do — do it."

**6** Now, standing nearby were six large stone water jars — the kind used for Jewish purification rituals. Each one could hold somewhere between twenty and thirty gallons. **7** Jesus told the servants, "Fill those jars up with water." So they filled them right to the brim. **8** Then he said, "Now dip some out and take it to the man running the banquet." They did.

**9** When the master of ceremonies tasted it — and it was wine now, though he had no clue where it came from (the servants who'd drawn the water knew, of course) — he pulled the groom aside **10** and said, "What are you doing? Everyone serves the good wine first, and then once people have had plenty to drink, they bring out the cheap stuff. But you? You've saved the best for last!"

**11** This was the first sign Jesus performed. It happened in Cana of Galilee, and it revealed his glory — and his followers put their trust in him.

**12** After that, he went down to Capernaum with his mother, his brothers, and his followers. They stayed there for a few days.

---

**13** The Jewish Passover festival was approaching, so Jesus headed up to Jerusalem. **14** When he got to the temple, he found it had become a marketplace — people selling cattle, sheep, and pigeons, and money changers sitting at their tables doing business. **15** So he grabbed some cords, made a whip out of them, and drove the whole lot out of the temple — sheep, cattle, all of them. He scattered the money changers' coins across the floor and flipped their tables over. **16** To the pigeon sellers he said, "Get all of this out of here! Stop turning my Father's house into a shopping mall!"

**17** His followers remembered that it's written: "Passion for your house will consume me."

**18** The Jewish leaders confronted him: "What gives you the right to do this? Show us a sign to prove your authority."

**19** Jesus answered, "Tear down this temple, and I'll raise it back up in three days."

**20** They scoffed, "It took forty-six years to build this temple, and you're going to rebuild it in three days?"

**21** But the temple he was talking about was his own body. **22** Later, after he'd been raised from the dead, his followers remembered he'd said this — and they believed the Scripture and the words Jesus had spoken.

**23** While Jesus was in Jerusalem for the Passover festival, a lot of people saw the signs he was doing and believed in him. **24** But Jesus didn't entrust himself to them — he knew what people were really like. **25** He didn't need anyone to tell him about human nature. He could see right through people.

## Notes

- v.4: The Greek *τί ἐμοὶ καὶ σοί, γύναι* is a Semitic idiom roughly meaning "what concern is that to you and me?" Rendered as a gentle deflection rather than a rebuke, since the tone is debated. "Mom" for *γύναι* — while the Greek literally means "woman," it was a respectful address, not dismissive. "Mom" captures the relational context better for modern English readers.
- v.10: The master of ceremonies' remark is often read as comic relief — he genuinely doesn't understand what just happened, which makes the moment land with irony.
- v.16: "Shopping mall" for *οἶκον ἐμπορίου* ("house of trade/commerce") — chosen to convey the visceral commercialization Jesus was reacting against.
- v.19: *ναός* (naos) refers specifically to the inner sanctuary, not the broader temple complex (*ἱερόν/hieron*). John makes this distinction deliberately — Jesus is speaking about something more intimate than the building.
- v.24-25: The Greek uses a wordplay on *πιστεύω* — the people "believed" (ἐπίστευσαν) in him, but he did not "entrust" (ἐπίστευεν) himself to them. Same verb, different directions. The paraphrase tries to preserve this contrast.
