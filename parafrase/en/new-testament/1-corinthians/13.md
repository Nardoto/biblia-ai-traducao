# 1 Corinthians 13

**1** If I speak in the tongues of humans and of angels but don't have loveᵃ, I'm just a loud gong or a clanging cymbal. **2** If I have the gift of prophecy, and I understand all mysteries and all knowledge, and if I have faith strong enough to move mountains, but don't have love — I am nothing. **3** If I give away everything I own and hand over my body to be burned,ᵇ but don't have love, I gain nothing.

**4** Love is patient. Love is kind. It doesn't envy. It doesn't boast. It isn't arrogant. **5** It doesn't dishonor others. It isn't self-seeking. It isn't easily angered. It keeps no record of wrongs.ᶜ **6** Love doesn't delight in injustice but rejoices with the truth. **7** It bears all things, believes all things, hopes all things, endures all things.ᵈ

**8** Love never fails. But if there are prophecies, they'll come to an end. If there are tongues, they'll cease. If there is knowledge, it'll pass away. **9** For we know in part, and we prophesy in part, **10** but when what is completeᵉ comes, what is partial will pass away.

**11** When I was a child, I talked like a child, I thought like a child, I reasoned like a child. When I became an adult, I put childish ways behind me. **12** For now we see only a reflectionᶠ as in a mirror, but then face to face. Now I know in part; then I'll know fully, even as I am fully known.

**13** And now these three remain: faith, hope, and love. But the greatest of these is love.ᵍ

---

## Notes
- v.1a: ἀγάπη (agapē) — selfless, committed, active love; Paul's deliberate word choice distinguishes this from ἔρως (erōs, romantic love) and φιλία (philia, friendship); agapē is love that acts for the other's good regardless of feeling
- v.3b: Some manuscripts read καυχήσωμαι (kauchēsōmai, "that I may boast") instead of καυθήσομαι (kauthēsomai, "to be burned"); both are well-attested
- v.5c: οὐ λογίζεται τὸ κακόν (ou logizetai to kakon) — doesn't calculate, reckon, or keep an account of wrongs; an accounting metaphor
- v.7d: Paul uses four "all things" (πάντα, panta) clauses: στέγει (stegei, "bears/covers"), πιστεύει (pisteuei, "believes/trusts"), ἐλπίζει (elpizei, "hopes"), ὑπομένει (hypomenei, "endures/stands firm")
- v.10e: τὸ τέλειον (to teleion) — the complete, the perfect, the mature; refers to the fullness of God's future, not a specific event
- v.12f: δι' ἐσόπτρου ἐν αἰνίγματι (di' esoptrou en ainigmati) — "through a mirror in a riddle"; Corinth was famous for its bronze mirrors, which gave only imperfect reflections
- v.13g: μείζων δὲ τούτων ἡ ἀγάπη (meizōn de toutōn hē agapē) — love is greatest because faith and hope will be fulfilled, but love is eternal, reflecting God's own nature
