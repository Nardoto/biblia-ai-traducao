# 1 Corinthians 9

**1** Am I not free? Am I not an apostle? Haven't I seen Jesus our Lord?ᵃ Aren't you the result of my work in the Lord? **2** Even if I'm not an apostle to others, I certainly am to you! You are the proof of my apostleship in the Lord.

**3** This is my defense to those who sit in judgment on me. **4** Don't we have the right to food and drink? **5** Don't we have the right to take along a believing wife, like the other apostles and the Lord's brothers and Cephas?ᵇ **6** Or is it only Barnabas and I who must work for a living?

**7** Who serves as a soldier at their own expense? Who plants a vineyard and doesn't eat its grapes? Who tends a flock and doesn't drink the milk? **8** Am I saying this from a merely human perspective? Doesn't the law say the same thing? **9** For it's written in the law of Moses: "Don't muzzle an ox while it's treading out the grain."ᶜ Is God really concerned about oxen? **10** Isn't he actually saying this for our sake? Yes, it was written for us! The plowman should plow in hope, and the thresher should thresh in hope of sharing the harvest.

**11** If we've sown spiritual seed among you, is it too much to expect a material harvest from you? **12** If others have this right over you, don't we deserve it even more? But we didn't use this right. Instead, we put up with anything rather than put an obstacle in the way of Christ's good news.

**13** Don't you know that those who serve in the temple eat from the temple, and those who serve at the altar share in what's offered on the altar? **14** In the same way, the Lord ordered that those who preach the good news should earn their living from the good news.

**15** But I haven't used any of these rights. And I'm not writing this so that it'll be done for me now. I'd rather die than have anyone take away my reason for boasting!ᵈ **16** When I preach the good news, I have nothing to boast about, because I'm compelled to do it. Woe to me if I don't preach the good news! **17** If I do this voluntarily, I have a reward. If involuntarily, I've still been entrusted with a responsibility. **18** So what is my reward? Just this: that when I preach the good news, I offer it free of charge, not making full use of my rights as a preacher.

**19** Though I'm free from everyone, I've made myself a slave to everyone, to win as many as possible. **20** To the Jews I became like a Jew, to win the Jews. To those under the law I became like one under the law — though I myself am not under the law — to win those under the law. **21** To those outside the law I became like one outside the law — though I'm not outside God's law but under Christ's law — to win those outside the law. **22** To the weak I became weak, to win the weak. I've become all things to all people so that by every possible means I might save some. **23** I do all this for the sake of the good news, so that I may share in its blessings.

**24** Don't you know that in a race all the runners run, but only one gets the prize? Run in such a way as to get the prize! **25** Everyone who competes in the games goes into strict trainingᵉ. They do it to get a crown that won't last, but we do it to get a crown that will last forever. **26** So I don't run aimlessly; I don't fight like a boxer beating the air. **27** No, I discipline my body and bring it under control, so that after preaching to others, I myself won't be disqualifiedᶠ.

---

## Notes
- v.1a: ἑόρακα Ἰησοῦν τὸν κύριον (heoraka Iēsoun ton kyrion) — Paul refers to his encounter with the risen Christ (cf. Acts 9; 1 Cor 15:8), the foundation of his apostolic authority
- v.5b: Κηφᾶ (Kēpha) — the Aramaic name for Peter; Paul mentions him specifically, perhaps because the Corinthians revered him (cf. 1:12)
- v.9c: Paul quotes Deuteronomy 25:4 and interprets it as a principle with broader application beyond literal oxen
- v.15d: τὸ καύχημά μου (to kauchēma mou) — Paul's boast is that he preached without financial support, distinguishing himself from itinerant sophists who charged fees
- v.25e: ἐγκρατεύεται (enkrateuetai) — exercises self-control in all things; the Isthmian Games held near Corinth made this athletic metaphor especially vivid for the audience
- v.27f: ἀδόκιμος (adokimos) — disqualified, failing the test, rejected; Paul acknowledges the real possibility of his own failure
