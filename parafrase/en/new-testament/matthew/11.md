# Matthew 11

**1** After Jesus finished giving instructions to his twelve disciples, he moved on from there to teach and proclaim in their towns.

**2** Now John, who was in prison, heard about what the Messiah was doing. He sent word through his disciples **3** to ask, "Are you the one who's supposed to come, or should we be watching for someone else?"ᵃ

**4** Jesus answered them, "Go back and tell John what you're hearing and seeing: **5** blind people see, lame people walk, those with skin diseases are cleansed, deaf people hear, dead people are raised, and poor people are hearing good news.ᵇ **6** And how fortunate is anyone who doesn't stumble over me."

**7** As John's disciples were leaving, Jesus began talking to the crowds about John: "What did you go out to the wilderness to see? A reed swaying in the wind? **8** So what did you go out to see? A man dressed in fine clothes? People who wear fine clothes live in palaces. **9** So what did you really go out to see? A prophet? Yes — and I'm telling you, far more than a prophet. **10** This is the one about whom it's written: 'Look, I'm sending my messenger ahead of you, who'll prepare your path before you.'ᶜ

**11** "I'm telling you the truth: among those born of women, no one greater than John the Baptizer has ever appeared. Yet the least person in the kingdom of heaven is greater than he is.ᵈ **12** From the days of John the Baptizer until now, the kingdom of heaven has been under violent assault, and violent people are trying to seize it.ᵉ **13** All the Prophets and the Torah prophesied up until John. **14** And if you're willing to accept it, he's the Elijah who was supposed to come. **15** Whoever has ears, listen!

**16** "What can I compare this generation to? They're like children sitting in the marketplaces calling out to each other: **17** 'We played the flute for you and you didn't dance! We sang a funeral song and you didn't mourn!' **18** John came neither eating nor drinking, and they say, 'He's got a demon.' **19** The Son of Man came eating and drinking, and they say, 'Look at this man — a glutton and a drunk, a friend of tax collectors and sinners!'ᶠ But wisdom is proven right by her actions."

**20** Then Jesus began to denounce the towns where most of his powerful works had been done, because they didn't turn their lives around:

**21** "How terrible for you, Chorazin! How terrible for you, Bethsaida! If the powerful works done among you had been done in Tyre and Sidon, they would've turned their lives around long ago, sitting in sackcloth and ashes. **22** I'm telling you, it'll be more bearable for Tyre and Sidon on the day of judgment than for you. **23** And you, Capernaum — will you be lifted up to heaven? You'll be brought down to Hades!ᵍ If the powerful works done in you had been done in Sodom, it would still be standing today. **24** I'm telling you, it'll be more bearable for the land of Sodom on the day of judgment than for you."

**25** At that moment, Jesus said, "I praise you, Father — Lord of heaven and earth — because you've hidden these things from the wise and educated and revealed them to little children.ʰ **26** Yes, Father, because this is what pleased you.

**27** "Everything has been handed over to me by my Father. No one truly knows the Son except the Father, and no one truly knows the Father except the Son — and anyone the Son chooses to reveal him to.ⁱ

**28** "Come to me, all of you who are exhausted and weighed down, and I'll give you rest. **29** Put on my yoke and learn from me, because I'm gentle and humble in heart — and you'll find rest for your souls.ʲ **30** My yoke fits well and my load is light."

## Notes

- v.3a: John's question reveals real doubt. Imprisoned and facing death, the Messiah wasn't acting as expected — where was the political liberation and fiery judgment John had preached about?
- v.5b: Jesus's answer echoes Isaiah 35:5-6 and 61:1 — messianic signs. He lets the evidence speak rather than making a direct claim.
- v.10c: Combining Exodus 23:20 and Malachi 3:1.
- v.11d: A paradoxical statement — John is the greatest of the old era, but the new era of the kingdom surpasses everything that came before.
- v.12e: *biazetai* (βιάζεται) — this notoriously difficult verse can mean the kingdom is being forcefully advanced or violently attacked. The context of John's imprisonment suggests hostile opposition.
- v.19f: *philos telōnōn kai hamartōlōn* (φίλος τελωνῶν καὶ ἁμαρτωλῶν) — "friend of tax collectors and sinners." Meant as an insult, it's actually one of the most beautiful descriptions of Jesus.
- v.23g: *Hadēs* (ᾅδης) — the Greek realm of the dead, equivalent to Hebrew *Sheol*. Not the same as Gehenna.
- v.25h: *nēpioi* (νηπίοι) — "infants, little children." Not intellectual simplicity but openness and dependence.
- v.27i: This verse has a distinctly Johannine flavor — one of the most profound claims to divine relationship in the Synoptic Gospels.
- v.29j: *anapausin tais psychais hymōn* (ἀνάπαυσιν ταῖς ψυχαῖς ὑμῶν) — echoing Jeremiah 6:16. The "yoke" was a common Jewish metaphor for Torah study and religious obligation. Jesus's yoke replaces burdensome religious systems.
