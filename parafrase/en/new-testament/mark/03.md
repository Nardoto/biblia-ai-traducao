# Mark 3

**1** Jesus went into the synagogue again, and there was a man there with a withered hand. **2** They were watching Jesus closely to see if he'd heal the man on the Sabbath — they were looking for a reason to accuse him. **3** Jesus said to the man with the withered hand, "Stand up here in front of everyone."

**4** Then he asked them, "What does the Law allow on the Sabbath — doing good or doing harm? Saving a life or killing?" They said nothing. **5** Jesus looked around at them with anger, deeply grieved by how hardᵃ their hearts were, and told the man, "Stretch out your hand." He stretched it out, and his hand was completely restored. **6** The Pharisees walked out immediately and started plotting with the Herodiansᵇ about how to destroy Jesus.

**7** Jesus withdrew with his disciples to the lake, and a massive crowd followed him — people from Galilee, Judea, **8** Jerusalem, Idumea, beyond the Jordan, and from the area around Tyre and Sidon. This enormous crowd came because they heard about everything he was doing. **9** Jesus told his disciples to have a small boat ready for him so the crowd wouldn't crush him. **10** He'd healed so many people that everyone with diseases was pushing forward to touch him. **11** And whenever the unclean spirits saw him, they'd fall down before him and scream, "You are the Son of God!" **12** But he sternly ordered them not to reveal who he was.

**13** Jesus went up on a mountainside and called to him the ones he wanted — and they came. **14** He appointed twelveᶜ to be with him and to send them out to preach **15** and to have authority to drive out demons. **16** These are the twelve he appointed: Simon, whom he named Peter;ᵈ **17** James the son of Zebedee and his brother John — he nicknamed them Boanerges, which means "Sons of Thunder"; **18** Andrew, Philip, Bartholomew, Matthew, Thomas, James the son of Alphaeus, Thaddaeus, Simon the Zealot,ᵉ **19** and Judas Iscariot — the one who would betray him.

**20** Jesus went home, and once again such a crowd gathered that he and his disciples couldn't even eat. **21** When his family heard about all this, they went to take charge of him, because people were saying, "He's lost his mind."

**22** The scripture scholars who'd come down from Jerusalem were saying, "He's possessed by Beelzebul! He drives out demons by the ruler of the demons!" **23** So Jesus called them over and spoke to them in comparisons:ᶠ "How can Satan drive out Satan? **24** If a kingdom is divided against itself, that kingdom can't stand. **25** If a household is divided against itself, that household can't hold together. **26** And if Satan has risen up against himself and is divided, he can't stand either — he's finished.

**27** "In fact, nobody can break into a strong man's house and steal his stuff unless he first ties up the strong man. Then he can clean out the house.

**28** "I'm telling you the truth: every sin and every insultᵍ people speak can be forgiven. **29** But whoever insults the Holy Spirit will never be forgiven — they're guilty of an eternalʰ sin." **30** He said this because they were claiming, "He has an unclean spirit."

**31** Then Jesus's mother and brothers arrived. They stood outside and sent word in to call for him. **32** A crowd was sitting around him, and they told him, "Your mother and your brothers are outside looking for you." **33** Jesus replied, "Who are my mother and my brothers?" **34** He looked around at the people sitting in a circle around him and said, "Here — these are my mother and my brothers! **35** Whoever does God's will is my brother, my sister, and my mother."

## Notes

- v.5a: *pōrōsei* (πωρώσει) — "hardness/petrification"; the image is of something calcified, unable to feel
- v.6b: *Hērōdianoi* (Ἡρῳδιανοί) — supporters of Herod's dynasty; an unusual political alliance with the Pharisees, united only by opposition to Jesus
- v.14c: Some manuscripts add "whom he also named apostles" (*apostolous*, ἀποστόλους — "sent ones")
- v.16d: *Petros* (Πέτρος) — "Rock," from Aramaic *Kēphā* (Cephas)
- v.18e: *Kananaios* (Καναναῖος) — from Aramaic *qan'ān* meaning "zealous one," not referring to Canaan
- v.23f: *parabolais* (παραβολαῖς) — parables; literally "thrown alongside," comparisons using everyday images
- v.28g: *blasphēmiai* (βλασφημίαι) — slander, insult, defamation; traditionally "blasphemies"
- v.29h: Some manuscripts read "eternal sin" (*aiōniou hamartēmatos*); others read "never has forgiveness"
