# Mark 15

**1** First thing in the morning, the chief priests held a meeting with the elders, scripture scholars, and the entire council.ᵃ They bound Jesus, led him away, and handed him over to Pilate.

**2** Pilate asked him, "Are you the King of the Jews?" Jesus answered, "You say so."ᵇ **3** The chief priests were hurling accusation after accusation at him. **4** So Pilate asked him again, "Aren't you going to answer? Look how many charges they're bringing against you!" **5** But Jesus still gave no answer — which amazed Pilate.

**6** Now it was the custom at the festival to release one prisoner — whoever the crowd requested. **7** A man named Barabbas was in prison with the rebels who had committed murder during the uprising. **8** The crowd came up and began asking Pilate to do what he usually did for them. **9** Pilate answered, "Do you want me to release 'the King of the Jews' for you?" **10** He knew the chief priests had handed Jesus over out of envy. **11** But the chief priests stirred up the crowd to have him release Barabbas instead.

**12** Pilate asked them again, "Then what do you want me to do with the one you call the King of the Jews?" **13** They shouted back, "Crucify him!" **14** "Why? What crime has he committed?" Pilate asked. But they just screamed louder, "Crucify him!"

**15** Wanting to satisfy the crowd, Pilate released Barabbas to them. He had Jesus flogged and handed him over to be crucified.

**16** The soldiers led Jesus into the courtyard — the Praetorium — and called together the whole cohort.ᶜ **17** They dressed him in a purple robe, twisted together a crown of thorns, and placed it on him. **18** And they began saluting him: "Hail, King of the Jews!" **19** They kept striking his head with a reed, spitting on him, and kneeling before him in mock worship. **20** When they'd finished mocking him, they stripped off the purple robe, put his own clothes back on him, and led him out to crucify him.

**21** They forced a passerby named Simon — from Cyrene, the father of Alexander and Rufusᵈ — to carry his cross. He was coming in from the countryside.

**22** They brought Jesus to the place called Golgotha,ᵉ which means "Place of the Skull." **23** They offered him wine mixed with myrrh, but he didn't take it. **24** They crucified him and divided up his clothes, casting lots to decide who got what.ᶠ

**25** It was nine in the morningᵍ when they crucified him. **26** The written charge against him read: THE KING OF THE JEWS.

**27** They crucified two rebels with him — one on his right and one on his left.ʰ

**29** People passing by hurled insults at him, shaking their heads and saying, "Ha! You who were going to destroy the temple and rebuild it in three days — **30** save yourself and come down from the cross!" **31** The chief priests and scripture scholars mocked him the same way among themselves: "He saved others but can't save himself! **32** Let the Messiah, the King of Israel, come down from the cross right now — then we'll see it and believe!" Even those crucified with him were taunting him.

**33** At noon, darkness fell over the whole land, and it lasted until three in the afternoon.ⁱ **34** At three o'clock Jesus cried out in a loud voice, "Eloi, Eloi, lema sabachthani?"ʲ — which means, "My God, my God, why have you abandoned me?"

**35** Some of those standing nearby heard it and said, "Listen — he's calling Elijah!" **36** One of them ran, soaked a sponge in sour wine, put it on a reed, and offered it to him to drink, saying, "Wait — let's see if Elijah comes to take him down."

**37** Then Jesus let out a loud cry and breathed his last.ᵏ

**38** The curtain of the templeˡ was torn in two from top to bottom. **39** The centurion standing there facing him, seeing how he died, said, "Truly, this man was the Son of God!"ᵐ

**40** There were also women watching from a distance. Among them were Mary Magdalene, Mary the mother of James the younger and Joses, and Salome. **41** These women had followed him and served him when he was in Galilee. And many other women who had come up with him to Jerusalem were there too.

**42** It was Preparation Day — the day before the Sabbath — and evening was approaching. **43** Joseph of Arimathea, a respected member of the council who was himself waiting for God's kingdom, boldly went to Pilate and asked for Jesus's body. **44** Pilate was surprised that Jesus was already dead. He summoned the centurion and asked if Jesus had been dead long. **45** When he confirmed it with the centurion, he released the body to Joseph.

**46** Joseph bought a linen cloth, took down the body, wrapped it in the linen, and laid it in a tomb cut out of rock. Then he rolled a stone against the entrance of the tomb. **47** Mary Magdalene and Mary the mother of Joses watched where he was laid.

## Notes

- v.1a: *synedrion* (συνέδριον) — the Sanhedrin, the Jewish ruling council; this early morning session was needed because Jewish law required capital cases to be tried during daytime
- v.2b: *sy legeis* (σὺ λέγεις) — "You say so" or "You are saying it"; an ambiguous answer that neither fully affirms nor denies — contrast with the direct *egō eimi* ("I am") before the high priest in 14:62
- v.16c: *speira* (σπεῖρα) — a Roman cohort, theoretically 600 soldiers, though in practice the number varied
- v.21d: Mark names Alexander and Rufus, suggesting they were known to his audience; Rufus may be the person Paul greets in Romans 16:13
- v.22e: *Golgotha* (Γολγοθᾶ) — Aramaic *gulgaltā*, "skull"; the Latin equivalent is *calvaria*, giving us "Calvary"
- v.24f: Echoing Psalm 22:18
- v.25g: *hōra tritē* (ὥρα τρίτη) — "the third hour," counting from sunrise (approximately 6 AM), so around 9 AM
- v.27h: Verse 28 ("And the Scripture was fulfilled that says, 'He was counted among the lawless'") appears in some manuscripts but is absent from the earliest ones
- v.33i: *hōra hektē...hōra enatē* — "sixth hour...ninth hour"; noon to 3 PM. Three hours of supernatural darkness
- v.34j: *Elōi, Elōi, lema sabachthani* (Ἐλωΐ, Ἐλωΐ, λεμὰ σαβαχθανί) — Aramaic, quoting Psalm 22:1. Mark preserves Jesus's own Aramaic words at this most agonizing moment. The bystanders' confusion with "Elijah" (*Ēlias*) works because the sounds are similar
- v.37k: *exepneusen* (ἐξέπνευσεν) — literally "breathed out"; Mark's simple, stark word for death
- v.38l: *katapetasma tou naou* (καταπέτασμα τοῦ ναοῦ) — the curtain of the inner sanctuary; whether this refers to the outer curtain (visible to all) or the inner curtain (separating the Holy Place from the Most Holy Place) is debated. Either way, it symbolizes broken barriers of access to God
- v.39m: *alēthōs houtos ho anthrōpos huios theou ēn* (ἀληθῶς οὗτος ὁ ἄνθρωπος υἱὸς θεοῦ ἦν) — the centurion's confession is the climax of Mark's gospel. The phrase lacks the Greek definite article ("a son of God" vs. "the Son of God"), though this is normal for predicate nouns. A Gentile soldier recognizes what the religious leaders refused to see
